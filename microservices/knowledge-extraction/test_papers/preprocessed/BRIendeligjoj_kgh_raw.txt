How has the contemporary development of sustainable buildings been inﬂuenced by the concept of ecological
modernization? Ecological modernization is a policy concept describing how environmental considerations are
increasingly being integrated into modern society’s institutions through, for example, new types of cooperation and
new applications of economic and market dynamics. Evidence is based on recent examples from politics and practice
in the construction sector in Denmark, where sustainable buildings have gone through great changes – from being
primarily isolated cases driven by enthusiasts and grassroots to being a more widespread, generally obtainable, and
integrated product. This is considered within the context of ecological modernization, along with the implications of
potential beneﬁts and drawbacks. Based on the concepts of governance, standardization, and visibility, it is found
that ecological modernization has penetrated in Danish sustainable buildings and has contributed to a positive
development. However, there are aspects of sustainable consumption to which this development does not relate,
including a constantly increasing demand for housing and the consequent increased demand for space heating.

Keywords: consumption, ecological modernization, governance, green building, housing, standardization, sustainable
building, Denmark

Ecological modernization of sustainable buildings

Ecological modernization is a policy concept suggesting
how environmental considerations are increasingly
being integrated into institutional, business, and per-
sonal decision-making, rather than being something
external and only within the environmental sector.
This integration happens by measuring and visualization
(Hajer, 1995; Mol and Sonnenfeld, 2000; Spaargaren,
2000) through new economic instruments and new
types of cooperation, such as more voluntary agreements
(Bostro¨m, 2003; Van Tatenhove and Leroy, 2003), and
through new roles for nation states, social movements,
and market actors (Van den Burg, 2006).

These elements are chosen on the basis of both the
theoretical concept of ecological modernization and
insight into present changes in the Danish construction
sector. Governance and visibility are elements from
ecological modernization that are also well known in
the construction sector. Standardization is central to
many processes within the construction sector, and
arguably a central part of the modernization process
in general (Farmer and Guy, 2005; Moore and
Engstrom, 2005) and, therefore, also of the ecological
modernization of sustainable buildings.

The ﬁrst section of the paper presents examples on the
politics of ecological modernization in general and in
the area of sustainable buildings. The second section
focuses on some of the recent building projects that
can be characterized as sustainable buildings in
Denmark. By analysing these building projects, it will
be argued that ecological modernization in many
ways promotes sustainability in buildings – but also
that in order to do this, it requires a more critical
approach.

Sustainable buildings are deﬁned herein as buildings
where environmental concerns are taken into account
in the construction process as well as in the operation
(including energy consumption) of the ﬁnal building.
A more precise deﬁnition of sustainable buildings,
however, is open to discussion. In a Danish context
the differentiation has been between ‘urban ecology’
and ‘environmental regulation’ as two fundamentally
different ways of perceiving sustainable buildings
(Jensen, 1994). Here urban ecology represents a holis-
tic and context-based understanding of sustainable
buildings, whereas environmental regulation refers to
a universal top-down bureaucratic approach to sus-
tainable buildings. A recent doctoral thesis concluded
that among actors in the construction business there
are very different views on which environmental indi-
cators should be used to assess sustainable buildings
(Dammann, 2004). The same plurality has been men-
tioned in an international context, for example that
the concept ‘sustainable buildings’ in practice covers
a range of different concepts based on technological,
social, aesthetic, commercial, and health issues, as
well as other logics (Guy and Farmer, 2001; Guy and
Osborne, 2001; Guy and Moore, 2005; Dammann
and Elle, 2006). One of the questions in the present
paper is thus whether ecological modernization of sus-
tainable buildings has led to different types of sustain-
able buildings with regard to how they are deﬁned,
planned, and designed, and with regard to the actors
involved.

Background
The concept of ecological modernization is a theory
that tries to explain how environmental considerations
are increasingly being integrated into society’s insti-
tutions and the changes caused by this process
(Hajer, 1995; Mol and Sonnenfeld, 2000; Spaargaren,
2000). The starting point is the belief that environ-
mental problems can be solved by the same actors
and institutions that originally created them (Beck,
1992). In other words, the ecological crisis should
not be solved by anti-modernism and anti-growth,
but
through
ecological
modernization
and
the
renewal of society, which can go hand in hand with
increased economic growth. Ecological modernization
can partly be seen as a counter-reaction to the percep-
tion that sustainability must build on self-sufﬁciency
and the ‘small is beautiful’ ideas which were prevalent
in the environmental movements of the 1970s and
1980s (Van den Burg, 2006). Instead, ecological mod-
ernization optimistically speaks for the ability of
society’s institutions to learn and renew themselves in
a win–win situation. Environmental challenges are
not necessarily barriers for growth and modernization,
but can instead be perceived as locomotives for growth
(Hajer, 1995). Within ecological modernization theory
there is an expectation that several sectors will partially
go through a modernization process which involves
more environmental production and consumption.
Hence, ecological modernization integrates sociologi-
cal, environmental, economic, and technological ques-
tions into one theory that suggests an understanding of
how the environmental challenges can be dealt with
(Spaargaren, 2000). Ecological modernization has
been used and developed in three stages (Mol and

In a Danish context the most important and well-
known part of the regulation is Building Regulations,
which have had energy savings as their main theme
since 1982. This illustrates that new types of regulation,
governance, network management, etc. are typically
taking place in parallel with the traditional top-down
regulation, or as governance in the shadows of the hier-
archy (Sharpf, 1994). Thus, sustainable buildings can
be seen as buildings where there are more ambitious
goals than those deﬁned in the Building Regulations,
and buildings where different types of planning and
cooperation models are being used.

Ecological modernization is a policy concept character-
izing the types of processes towards sustainability
rather than judging the results of these processes.
Thus, there is no direct link between ecological mod-
ernization and other concepts within sustainable build-
ings that are used to discuss the degree of sustainability
as, for instance, strong and weak sustainability (e.g.
Turner, 2006). In weak sustainability natural capital
can be run down if the man-made or human capital
is growing equally, whereas in strong sustainability
the natural capital must be constant or rising over
time. Ecological modernization can lead to strong as
well as weak sustainability depending on the actual
actors and processes.

The following section considers further how political
initiatives, which can be described as ecological mod-
ernization, have affected sustainable buildings. As
described above, the focus of the analysis of the eco-
logical modernization of sustainable buildings will be
under three headings: governance, standardization,
and visibility.

Political initiatives
Ecological modernization is a theory trying to explain
recent changes in policy. This means that those actually
developing and formulating the new policies are not
necessarily aware of whether their policy is in line
with what can be labelled ecological modernization.
In Danish policy, ecological modernization is not a
well-known term and there is no explicit goal to
follow this line. However, as will be described in this
paper, Danish policy in many respects actually does
follow it.

Governance
An important element of ecological modernization is
that it is closely connected to new types of state regu-
lation – a changed regulation that is often described
with the phrase ‘from government to governance’.
While the state used to see itself as more important
than the other actors in civil society and the market
and it regulated through legislation, enforcement
orders and prohibitions, it now increasingly deﬁnes
its role as an actor on an equal basis with others,
more often regulating through different institutional
arrangements such as voluntary agreements, network
cooperation, labelling schemes, etc. (Van Tatenhove
and Leroy, 2003). This means increasing self-regu-
lation of the environmental area through visibility of
resource consumption and environmental impact in
combination with ensuring that those who are being

(Bostro¨m, 2003). It also means that economic regu-

The principles of governance and the voluntary agree-
ments with the actors in the sector have not yet deliv-
ered substantial results in the Danish construction
sector. There is no doubt that legislation – in this
case the Danish Building Regulations – is still a stron-
ger instrument of regulation than voluntary agree-

how to work with the new types of tools and processes
takes time, and should be regarded as long-term edu-
cation of the actors in the construction sector. There-
fore, it is too early to assess the ﬁnal effect of the
voluntary tools.

In Denmark there are different examples of this change
in regulation and cooperation in the policies related to
buildings and the environment. One example is the
new product panels (Van Tatenhove and Leroy,
2003). In Denmark such panels were established in
different sectors at the end of the 1990s as part of the
product-oriented environmental policy of the Ministry
of the Environment. The building panel was a product
panel for the construction sector and its purpose was to
gather central actors around an action plan for sustain-
ability in the sector. It resulted in an ambitious plan
with a number of objectives and recommendations,
including the objective to increase energy efﬁciency
by a factor of ten over 30–35 years. One of the
measures was to make the actors in the building
sector use different tools, including the Miljørigtig Pro-
jektering (Manual on Environmental Management in
Project Design, or MEMPD) (Byggepanel, 2001). In
general the process showed that the clients were not
sufﬁciently motivated to outline environmental objec-
tives for their buildings and make use of the recommen-
dations of the building panel. Other studies have
shown that the use of tools to increase the environ-
mental effort in construction is very limited. Further-
more, the organization of the panel is debatable as all
members were researchers or consultants and there
were no representatives from the rest of the construc-
tion sector. This might be one reason that the work
of the building panel has had very limited inﬂuence,
even though the panel is often used as a reference
when discussing matters relating to sustainability in
the construction sector.

Standardization
Standardization – such as norms and voluntary agree-
ments – is closely connected to changed forms of gov-
ernance. As the use of ‘governance’ increases in public
regulation in line with less direct legislation, more
voluntary standards will be elaborated without a
formal authority to oblige the actors to use them
(Bostro¨m, 2003). A voluntary standard can be shaped
as a speciﬁc procedure that should be followed, e.g. a
tool, or it can be speciﬁc objectives that should be com-
plied with. The number of standards relating to sus-
tainable buildings has practically exploded during the
past years. In Denmark there are the MEMPD (which
has been further developed into the ABC planner),
the Building Environmental Assessment Tool (BEAT),
the Green Diploma, Green Accounting, the Greenbuild
Point System, The Swan (Nordic eco-label), Optobuild,
etc. Furthermore, many municipalities and private

Visibility
A central element in ecological modernization is the
attempt to make ecology measurable in order to inte-
grate it into programmes, policy and institutions
(Hajer, 1998). Among other things it builds on speciﬁ-
cations of ‘substance ﬂows’, i.e. ﬂows of energy, water
and material, which make it possible to measure the
environmental action and to map, compare, and set
targets. Monitoring and surveillance are performed
through tools such as LCA analyses, environmental
indicators, and eco-labels to make the environmental
impact visible in the everyday lives of consumers
(Spaargaren, 2000). This is expected to encourage con-
sumers to choose the environmentally friendliest
alternative.

There are various examples of how visibility is used in
an attempt to regulate and reduce the environmental
impact from buildings. One of these examples is indi-
vidual consumer metering of heating, electricity, fuel
gas and water, which came into law in 1995 (Act no.
362 on individual metering of consumption for electri-
city, gas, water and heating). The history of this act
referred to studies that document up to 20% savings
with individual metering and there is no doubt that
individual metering has helped reduce consumption
in many existing buildings, where savings around 15
and 20% are often obtained when individual metering
is established. However, in this case there is a link
between individual metering and individual payment,
so individual payment probably strongly contributes
to the savings as well as visibility of the consumption.

For large buildings (greater than 1500 m2), the ELO
scheme requires that an energy consultant registers
the consumption of energy and water in the building
annually, and for small buildings makes a report to
the owner with recommendations on how to reduce
consumption. Also in this case evaluations have
shown that relatively few building owners actually
follow these recommendations (The Danish Energy
Authority, 2001). There are different reasons for this.
One is that for rented accommodation owners do not
have sufﬁcient incentives to implement the energy-
saving measures as the beneﬁt primarily goes to the
tenants through lower energy and water bills. Other
reasons are that the energy-saving measures compete
with other types of improvements to the property,

Concerning standardization, there is no doubt that
demands for documentation and quantitative objec-
tives are increasingly gaining ground. An essential dis-
cussion – also internationally – is whether increasing
use of certain standards or assessment tools will lead
to an alignment in the perception of sustainable build-
ing (Guy and Moore, 2005), and the question is
whether this development is desirable. The present
paper points to the fact that in Denmark today there
is no one standard that has come through, instead
there are a number of different tools and assessment
methods on the market that reﬂect the plurality in the
perception of sustainable buildings, a plurality within
sustainable building which the authors have spoken
of approvingly as it reﬂects different understandings
and approaches to sustainable buildings represented
in the different building projects. Concerning the
effect of the tools it can be argued that they should
not just be seen as a way to increase the environmental
contents of the projects; they can also form some kinds
of ‘story lines’ that in a simple and clear way explain
what sustainability is in this speciﬁc building and
thereby also qualify the process and the decision-
making in the building process.

In relation to ecological modernization there are argu-
ments both in favour of and against this normalization
of the architecture of sustainable buildings. On the one
hand the idea is to normalize sustainability by integrat-
ing it into all processes and institutions of modern
society and thus the goal is reached if people can buy
an
ordinary
house
and
be
sustainable
without
knowing it. On the other hand, the theory of ecological
modernization, especially in the third stage, empha-
sizes the importance of consumers actively demanding
green products and technologies as a main driver for a
more sustainable everyday life in households. If sus-
tainability increasingly gets normalized and integrated
into existing procedures and products, it will appar-
ently reduce the room for consumers’ individual
choice. Therefore, it can be argued that the strategy
of making sustainability invisible, which is based on
a supply-oriented approach, takes consumers’ choices
and preferences of sustainability into account to a
much smaller degree, and therefore runs the risk of dis-
tancing itself from the consumers.

Visibility of consumption for consumers is the third
element in this analysis of ecological modernization
in buildings and construction processes. When visi-
bility is connected to individual payment of the
resource consumption of the households, it can lead
to great savings and also to environmental beneﬁts.
Another element of visibility is the idiom of the build-
ings. There is a clear tendency to build sustainable
housing that can sell on market terms to ‘ordinary
people’. This means that sustainability is made far
more invisible and that the sustainable buildings look
like ‘normal’ buildings. This can, on the one hand, be
a relief compared with earlier very grassroots-oriented
architecture or the cases where new technical solutions
were simply added to existing buildings. On the other
hand, this may hide the debate of sustainability and
thus of alternatives to the dominant standard building.
Therefore, it can be argued that there is still an import-
ant role for the grassroots to play. Neither Buildings
Regulations nor the theories of ecological moderniz-
ation have any suggestions about how to deal with
growing housing consumption and growing housing
prices which force people to live far from their work-
places and thus depend on long daily commutes.

Conclusion
In the discussion of how, and to what extent, ecological
modernization has affected sustainable buildings in
Denmark,
selected
policy
actions
and
practical
examples of sustainable buildings have been reviewed.
In both cases the writing has been thematized by the
three elements related to ecological modernization
that the authors ﬁnd essential for sustainable buildings:
governance, standardization, and visibility. Overall it
can be concluded that development of sustainable
buildings in Denmark in many ways follows the
paths of ecological modernization.

In relation to governance, the paper argues that the
increasing degree of voluntary involvement in deﬁning
committing goals and initiatives still lacks results.
The construction sector has not yet been able to
develop convincing objectives for sustainability, and
for this reason the signiﬁcant improvements in sustain-
ability of the building activities are still being derived


Ecological modernization of sustainable buildings

For this reason the grassroots approach as seen in
Friland and other alternative settlements still has a
mission to inspire and create debate about what
should be understood as sustainable buildings – inspi-
ration that in the end will create further dynamics for
ecological modernization.
