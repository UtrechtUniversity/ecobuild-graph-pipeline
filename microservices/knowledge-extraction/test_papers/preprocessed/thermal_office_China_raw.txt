Faced with urban high-density environment, current energy-saving measures are focused on climatic
design and technical improvement, which might not align well with China’s national policies on
extending the longevity of high-rise buildings to realize life cycle sustainability. Therefore, this paper
explores energy-efﬁcient strategies for tall ofﬁce buildings in response to the time-varying operational
parameters to realize spatial malleability from industrialized perspective. The results from a case study
show that, ﬁrst, the model of ofﬁce þ multi-courtyards has positive impact on climatic adaptation, in
terms of ventilation, air temperature, thermal radiation environment, and effectiveness of thermal en-
ergy conservation. Second, the average energy efﬁciency rate of the model of ofﬁce þ multi-courtyards is
from 12.8% to 4.7% with the interior space coverage of 20%e80%. Third, when the interior space coverage
ranges from 50% to 60%, the average energy efﬁciency rate appears substantial ﬂuctuation from 10.3% to
6.5% and appears the change point when the interior space coverage is 58%.

1. Introduction

The built environment is a signiﬁcant component of world en-
ergy consumption and carbon emissions (Chen et al., 2008; Mi
et al., 2018). The urban sprawl, to which global warming is
strictly related, has caused a series of environmental hazards that
are well-known but difﬁcult to tackle: the Urban Heat Island effect
(UHI) is one of them (Yang et al., 2011; Susca et al., 2011). According
to the statistics, China’s average urbanization rate is close to 60%,
while the urbanization rate of the ﬁrst-tier cities has exceeded
85%.1 Besides, 87% of skyscrapers at the world level appear in China,
making it become the developing centers of urban high-rise and
high-density areas and bringing strong negative impact of energy
consumption and carbon emissions (Mi et al., 2015; Tan et al., 2016;

It is thus clear that the measures taken on tall buildings’ thermal
energy consumption in various countries are often through space
adjustment (interior space and outdoor public space), architectural
forms optimization, courtyards implantation, and greenery systems
regulation. However, these methods could improve the energy ef-
ﬁciency through “repair” for buildings, but not fundamentally meet
occupants’ demand on spatial malleability with extending the
longevity of high-rise buildings from the industrialized perspective.
In addition, China has a vast expanse of land with complicated
topographies and different types of climate, therefore the charac-
teristics of energy consumption of tall ofﬁce buildings in different
regions are diverse. Facing intensive urban environment, the
impact of factors, such as function sustainability, health perception
and natural environment contact, on energy efﬁciency and thermal
performance of high-rise buildings are inevitably needed to be
considered.
Based on China’s national policy on promoting building indus-
trialization, building separation system is considered as a funda-
mental condition. This includes constant system (support structure,
public facilities tube well, vertical transportation) and variable
system (building envelope, domestic facilities), which provides the
possibility to realize the adjustable and variable interior space with
free layout on “secondary ground” (Habraken, 2003). Speciﬁcally,
the long-term effect and the complexity of building separation
system as a technical and structural support makes adjustable
space modules and different space coverage in freestyle layout on
“secondary ground”. On the one hand, the service life prolongs
because of the separation of building components with permanent
system and the ones that need to be updated frequently. This is an
effective response to the changes of built environmental conditions,
and the progress in the aspects of sustainability and adaptation. On
the other hand, “secondary ground” is like vacant lot owed by users,
in which the joint change of interior partition walls and exterior
enclosures improves the spatial variability, which allows the
physical artefact a level of malleability in response to changing
operational parameters over time (Schmidt et al., 2010). In this way,
the designers, builders and manufacturers only provide sugges-
tions and choices according to users’ needs. This change also echoes
with the target of industrial construction with the characteristics of
longevity and low-carbon footprint (Buyle et al., 2013; Li and
Colombier, 2009). As for variable layout for building carbon emis-
sions, Klemecic et al. (2012) use double-bottom-line to evaluate the
relationship between carbon footprint and building longevity,
including long-term re-use value, to analyze the targeted tall
building in Chicago, from the perspective of macro-economics. The
results suggest that open ﬂoor plans and ﬂexible space, within
limits, can change over time and longer building durations.

classiﬁed as attached, semi-enveloped, integrated, linear, and

expansion for various parts of a building (Wan and Yik, 2004).
Hertzberger (2005) proposes the Polysemic Space that corresponds
to “ﬂexibility”. Thus, the spatial characteristics have the adaptive
capacity to transform according to the required function. Addi-
tionally, Kurokawa (2000) puts forward the “Intermediary Space
theory”, which holds that space with intermediary characteristics
can still integrate the opposite objects in a dynamic system. Thus,

2.1.2. Measurement instrumentation and parameters
The measurements are taken in dates with typical weather (high
temperatures and high humidity) of Nanning City in hot summer.
This paper describes the process of measurement and the data
extracted from the experiment. The measurement is set on
different ofﬁce þ courtyard space of a tall ofﬁce building. Besides,
the weather condition of the lasting 8 days during the test is shown
in Fig. 4.
The previous day of the ﬁrst measurement was a sunny summer
day. During the measurement, day 1, day 2, day 5, day 8 are sunny,
and the value of the total horizontal solar radiation exceeds 400 W/
㎡from 12:00 to 14:00; day 3 is cloudy; day 6, and day 7 are
overcast with short rain. Therefore, day 1 and day 2 (sunny), day 3
(cloudy), day 6 and day 7 (overcast) can be taken as effective data.
During the experiment, daily highest and lowest air temperature is
35 C and 19 C in sunny days, respectively. The peak of air tem-
perature appears at around 13:00 to 15:00 and the minimum value
appears at around 5:00 to 7:00. Moreover, the relative humidity of
the air decreases proportionally with the increase air temperature,
and the numerical ﬂuctuation ranges from 45% to 103%. If there is
no thermal regulation in a hot-humid climate zone in summer,
people may feel very stuffy (Aktacir et al., 2010).
The following parameters for each measurement point are
measured at the height of 1.2 m above the ground: air temperature,
relative humidity, black bulb temperature, air velocity and surface
temperature as shown in Table 2. The sensors are mounted on a
three-legged stand as shown in Fig. 3. Surface temperatures around
the measurement points are observed using an infrared camera. A
thermal index, Globe Temperature, is measured by standard globe
temperature probe (Taleghani et al., 2013). Table 2 brieﬂy describes
the instruments used in the measurement. These instruments are
calibrated in the laboratory. One measurement for a point is carried
out at 1-h interval and ﬁnishes in 15 min. Lastly, a Hobo weather
station is set up to collect the meteorological parameters which
include the outdoor air temperature, relative humidity and solar
radiation during the test.

2.2.2. Description of implementation schema of energy
consumption simulation
The previous ﬁeld measurement and numerical simulation
focus on estimating the climatic adaptation of the mode of
ofﬁce þ multi-courtyards from the perspective of cooling effect and
energy efﬁciency. This is a necessary step for evaluating the climatic
adaptation of the layout. Besides, it needs to further discuss the
strategy for extending the spatial service life. The simulation
mentioned above cannot be served as a reference for users’ when
they participate in the process of industrial construction e, because
in this simulation, the spatial, structural, and service strategies
cannot change operational parameters over time, to improve the
spatial malleability. Thus, an EnergyPlus model predictive control
(MPC) system is developed by using Matlab. The system can be
applied for predicting and calculating the energy varying in in-
dustrial construction. To predict the energy efﬁciency with time-
varying operational parameters, the model predictive control sys-
tem has been further developed. Numerous studies have estab-
lished
through
numerical
simulation
to
demonstrate
MPC’s
effectiveness in predicting energy consumption (Zhao et al., 2014;
Tam et al., 2018; Sayadi et al., 2018). However, most of the studies
focus on active HVAC systems, such as variable air volume (VAV)
and radiant heating systems (Henze et al., 2008). In order to apply
the system in the ﬁeld of spatial malleability, the following study
develops a control scheme using the mode of ofﬁce þ multi-
courtyards as a test-bed to predict its energy performance based on
the time-varying parameters to meet users’ continuous changing
requirements of space.
The control system is established in the Matlab environment
and connected with the EnergyPlus by writing and reading the text
ﬁle. Thus the EnergyPlus model is directly co-simulated and opti-
mized with the control law in the Matlab environment. This pro-
gram meets the requirement of predicting and reﬂecting the
change of energy consumption by controlling the operational pa-
rameters of the space and layout varied by users’ needs over time.
As output of the simulation, the continuously changed layouts
and usage space coverage can be visualized on the 2D models under
the control of parameters (see the right corner of Fig. 5). Besides,
the energy consumption and energy efﬁciency rate corresponding
to the continuously changed layouts can be predicted and calcu-
lated dynamically over time.
The above-mentioned tall ofﬁce building is used as the building
model for numerical simulation study. The interior space coverage
of “secondary ground” is assumed to be 20%, 30%, 40%, 50%, 60%,
70%, and 80%, respectively. Here, the interior space coverage is
deﬁned to be percentage of the ﬂoor’s area which is taken by ofﬁce
room. Schematic description of a case is illustrated in Fig. 5.
