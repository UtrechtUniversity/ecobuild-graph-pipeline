A holistic design approach for residential net-zero energy buildings: A case
study in Singapore

T

Lan Lan⁎, Kristin L. Wood, Chau Yuen

Singapore University of Technology and Design (SUTD), 8 Somapah Road, Singapore 487372, Singapore

A R T I C L E I N F O

A B S T R A C T

The concept of net-zero energy buildings is an important element and dimension of the sustainable built en-
vironment. This paper introduces a holistic design approach for residential net-zero energy building (NZEB) by
adopting the Triple Bottom Line (TBL) principles: social, environmental, and ﬁnancial. The social need is
mapped to human comfort and nature contact (i.e. thermal comfort achieved by natural cooling, and visual
comfort achieved by daylighting); the environmental need is mapped to energy eﬃciency; and the ﬁnancial need
is mapped to life cycle cost (LCC). Multi-objective optimizations are conducted in two phases: the ﬁrst phase
optimizes the utilization rate of natural cooling and daylighting, and the second phase optimizes energy eﬃ-
ciency and LCC. Sensitivity analysis is conducted to identify the most inﬂuential variables in the optimization
process. The approach is applied to the design of residential NZEBs in a tropical country, Singapore. The po-
tential of building residential NZEBs in Singapore is evaluated with two typical residential building types: a
landed house and apartment building. The required capacity of a renewable energy system (RES) is calculated.
Results show that while it is achievable to build a net-zero energy landed house with only rooftop solar panels, it
is much more diﬃcult to achieve net-zero energy for apartment buildings. Further design considerations and
analysis show that for a 25-ﬂoor H-shaped residential building with a solar panel integrated facade, the produced
electricity is able to meet the energy demand of up to 19 ﬂoors. Findings and derived insights from the case study
show that although some variables need to be carefully selected to balance daylighting and natural cooling, the
two objectives do not always contradict each other regarding certain variables. Similarly, environmental aims
and economic aims do not always contradict each other on certain variables. Also, the social aims do not
contradict environmental and economic aims, as the ﬁndings show that designing for daylighting and natural
cooling contributes to the improvement of energy eﬃciency and cost eﬀectiveness. These results provide a
framework and modeled cases for design insights, parametric design, and trade-oﬀanalysis toward sustainable
and livable built structures.

2. Method

2.1. Triple bottom lines

The TBL principles are used to evaluate sustainability using three
factors: Social, Environmental, and Economic (Hall, 2011). They are
also called the ”3Ps”, which stands for People, Planet, and Proﬁt. For a
project to be sustainable, it has to address people's needs and be desired
by them. It also needs to be responsible for the planet and the future of
human beings. Last but not least, it has to bring proﬁt or economic
viability, so that it can attract business and investment. With the pri-
mary aim of being sustainable, NZEBs should align with the TBL prin-
ciples.

towards a building is human comfort, which consists of thermal com-
fort, visual comfort, acoustic comfort, and indoor air quality (IAQ)
(Autodesk Knowledge Network, 2018). People also have the need of
contact with nature in a building. Many studies have shown that access
to nature signiﬁcantly aﬀects human health and productivity (Kellert,
Heerwagen, & Mador, 2011). Nature contact may take many forms in
the built environment, such as plants and greenery in buildings, views
out of windows, daylighting, and natural wind.
In this work, the aim is to optimize selected and critical factors in
terms of visual and thermal comfort that is achievable by natural
means, i.e. daylighting and natural cooling. There are many beneﬁts of
daylighting. Physiologically, daylight is an eﬀective stimulant to the
human visual system and the human circadian system. Psychologically,
daylight and a view outdoors are highly desired and, as a consequence,
may have beneﬁts for human well-being (Boyce & Raynham, 2009).
Natural cooling in NZEB also beneﬁts human health. Air-conditioning
brings thermal comfort to people, but over long time durations, it may
cause many health problems including nasal issues, headaches, itchy
eyes, diﬃculty breathing and dizziness due to poor IAQ. Seppänen and
Fisk (2002) found an increase in prevalence of sick building syndrome
(SBS) between 30% and 200% in air-conditioned buildings compared to
natural ventilation buildings.
Furthermore, study in Gou, Gamage, Lau, and Lau (2018) revealed
that occupants living in naturally ventilated buildings in the tropics
were exposed to higher operative temperatures than the ASHRAE
comfort standard for naturally conditioned spaces. However, they were
more likely to accept such conditions. Therefore, it would be re-
commended to design for natural ventilation for buildings in the tro-
pics, such as Singapore.
Based on the TBL principles, this paper proposes a holistic design
approach for residential NZEB, which aligns with the TBL principles by
optimizing the utilization of daylighting and natural cooling, energy
eﬃciency and cost eﬀectiveness, as shown in Fig. 1.

2.3. Design optimization process

In this work, JEPlus+EA (JEPlus, 2018), a multi-objective optimi-
zation tool based on NSGA-II, is coupled with EnergyPlus to explore
optimized solutions and to seek insights into the design parametric
space that leads to zero energy buildings. NSGA-II is one of the most
eﬃcient multi-objective evolutionary algorithms (Delgarm, Sajadi,
Delgarm, & Kowsary, 2016; Yu, Li, Jia, Zhang, & Wang, 2015). It at-
tempts to perform global optimization by generating oﬀsprings using a
speciﬁc type of crossover and mutation, and then selecting a new po-
pulation according to non-dominated sorting and crowding distance
comparison (Deb, Pratap, Agarwal, & Meyarivan, 2002). In this work,
population means a set of candidate solutions in the current generation.
Each candidate solution (i.e. chromosome) comprises a set of design
variables (i.e. genes), which determines building and system properties.
EnergyPlus (EnergyPlus, 2018) is one of the most commonly used
whole building simulation software. It has three basic components: a
heat and mass balance simulation module, a building systems simula-
tion module, and a simulation manager. EnergyPlus implements de-
tailed building physics algorithms for heat transfer (radiation, convec-
tion, and conduction), air and moisture transfer, light distribution, and
water ﬂows. It is able to model a broad range of building and me-
chanical system conﬁgurations and conditions (Crawley et al., 2001).
The proposed process consists of two phases, as shown in Fig. 2. The
ﬁrst phase aims to minimize natural cooling thermal discomfort time
(Otd) and ineﬀective daylighting time (Odi). In Phase 1, an EnergyPlus
model is ﬁrst created with basic building geometry, construction in-
formation, internal heat gains, airﬂow network, and daylighting. In
JEPlus, passive design variables (as shown in Fig. 2) are deﬁned and
linked to the EnergyPlus model. NSGA-II based optimization is per-
formed in JEPlus+EA to minimize Otd and Odi. The data of the opti-
mization results is imported to Trade Space Visualizer (Trade Space
Visualizer, 2018) for visualization and analysis of the trade space. From
the trade space, best candidates with long eﬀective daylighting time
and natural cooling time are selected for further optimization and
analysis. In addition, sensitivity analysis is conducted in SimLab
(SimLab, 2018) to identify the most inﬂuential design variables. Based

3. Case study

To test the eﬀectiveness of the proposed methodology, a case study
is conducted to design residential NZEBs in a tropical country,
Singapore. In this section, the climate and residential building types of
Singapore are introduced ﬁrst, and then the design and optimization
process is explained.

3.1. Climate and residential building types of Singapore

Singapore is 1-degree north of the equator and has a tropical rain-
forest climate, which is characterized by a relatively uniform tem-
perature and pressure, high humidity, and abundant rainfall. The 24-
hour mean temperature, humidity and sunshine hours of each month
are shown in Fig. 3. The 24-hour mean temperature varies from 26 °C in
December and January, to 27.8 °C in May and June. The daily tem-
perature range has a minimum usually not falling below 23–25 °C
during the night and maximum not rising above 31–33 °C during the
day. Therefore, it may be possible to achieve thermal comfort without
air-conditioning. The humidity is high, ranging from 82.6% to 86.7%.
In Singapore, wind directions are mainly from northerly to north-
easterly during the Northeast Monsoon (December to March) and
southerly to south-easterly during the Southwest Monsoon (June to
September). Wind strength is greater during the Northeast Monsoon.

3.2.1. Phase 1
This case study is based on a hypothetical ﬂoor plan of a typical
terraced house in Singapore as shown in Fig. 4. The house has three
ﬂoors with total ﬂoor area of 306 m2 and ﬂoor height of 3.5 m. It has
one living room, four bedrooms, four bathrooms, two study rooms, and
one kitchen.
Exterior windows are located in the front and backside walls. The
window type in the model is the sliding window, which is the most
commonly used in Singapore. The discharge coeﬃcient is ﬁxed at 0.6,
which is a commonly used value for the discharge coeﬃcient of a sharp-
rimmed large opening (Flourentzou, Van der Maas, & Roulet, 1998).
When fully opened, the eﬀective ventilation ratio of sliding window is
0.5.
The side walls are set to be adiabatic because they are shared by two
households (terraced house type), and it is assumed to have similar
thermal condition and no heat transfer. The internal ceilings are also
assumed to be adiabatic. The front and backside exterior walls and the
roof are constructed with 100mm thick brick with 20mm thick cement
plasters on both sides. The resulting U −value is 2.76 W/m2 k.
Additional insulation is set as a design variable in Phase 1 optimization.

The sensitivity indexes used in this work are the Partial Correlation
Coeﬃcient (PCC) and Standardized Rank Regression Coeﬃcients
(SRRC). PCC is one of the most suitable indexes for building models
considering accuracy and number of inputs (Nguyen & Reiter, 2015).
The PCC shows the strength of the correlation between an output Y and
an associated input vector Xi, which was cleaned oﬀany eﬀect due to
the correlation between the vector Xi and other input vectors. The SRRC
is based on regression analysis, but is improved by applying the rank
transformation of the input and output values (i.e. replacing these va-
lues with their ranks). PCC is suitable for linear models, while SRRC can
be used for non-linear but monotonic functions among inputs and
outputs. The PCC and SRRC tend to supplement each other in mea-
suring sensitivity. Therefore, in this study, both PCC and SRRC are used
to provide complex and accurate evaluations.

SRRC results show good consistency. It is found that overhang projec-
tion, window type, and WWR are the most inﬂuential variables in
daylighting performance. For natural cooling performance, window
status is found to be the dominant factor over others.

4.1. Findings and derived design insights from the case study

4.1.1. Do thermal comfort and visual comfort aims necessarily contradict
each other
Generally, natural cooling requires large overhang and low SHGC of
glazing to reduce solar heat gain, high heat conduction and convection
of facade if room air temperature (increased due to internal heat gains)
is higher than outdoor temperature, or low heat conduction and con-
vection of facade if room air temperature is lower than outdoor tem-
perature. Daylighting requires large glazing area, small overhang, and
high VT to increase the illuminance level. Therefore, some variables
(e.g. overhang projection) have contradictory impacts on natural
cooling and daylighting, while other variables (e.g. WWR) may have
same or opposed eﬀects on the two objectives depending on the indoor
and outdoor condition. The results of the case study (as shown in 7 )
show that among the most inﬂuential variables, overhang projection
has positive correlation with thermal comfort while negative correla-
tion with visual comfort. Window type (U −value|SHGC|VT) has ne-
gative correlation with thermal comfort while positive correlation with
visual comfort. However, WWR has positive correlation with both
thermal comfort and daylighting. Therefore, well balanced overhang
projection and window properties should be chosen by multi-objective
optimization. And WWR should be maximized to optimize daylighting
and natural cooling.

4.1.4. What are the diﬀerences between the results of the landed house and
apartment building
The main diﬀerences between the landed house model and the
apartment building model are structural diﬀerences (i.e. diﬀerences in
number of ﬂoors, sizes and location of diﬀerent room types, and
building shapes), and occupant density. Those diﬀerences result in the

4.3. Comparisons with non-tropical climate results

As shown in Table 8, most of the existing studies were conducted in
the temperate zone (where temperatures can change greatly between
summer and winter), but the research in tropical climate is lacking.
The housing types in the studied locations are mostly detached
single-family houses. On the contrary, due to the high population
density in Singapore, the majority of people live in high-rise apartment
buildings, and most of the landed houses are the strata type.

4.1.5. Impacts of occupant behavior on NZEB performances
It has long been known that occupant behavior has great impact on
energy use in buildings. As stated by Stern (2011), engineers and ar-
chitects are designing buildings to have the desired energy properties,
but such buildings will not become commonplace unless people want to
occupy them and will not achieve their technical potential if the oc-
cupants behave in ways that counteract the designers’ intent. Therefore,
it is important to adopt approaches which address the range of system-
level, holistic objectives, such as Design Innovation (DI) for user ex-
periences (Camburn et al., 2017; Lan et al., 2018). By adopting DI and
TBL principles, the proposed approach integrates daylighting and nat-
ural cooling in the design of NZEB with the aim to improve occupants’
satisfaction. The integration of these features also helps to improve
building energy eﬃciency. However, it is important to further guar-
antee the desired user behaviors of utilizing daylighting and natural
cooling. Therefore, eﬀorts from other domains, such as behavioral sci-
ence, are also important for NZEB performances.

Carlucci et al. (2015) optimized thermal and visual comfort for a
detached single family house in Southern Italy. The results showed that
highly insulated and medium weighted walls and roofs (100% of cases),
less insulated ﬂoors, glazing units with a low thermal transmittance and
a low solar factor, shading control, and control strategies for summer
night cooling were desired for optimized thermal performance. Visual
comfort required similar designs as thermal comfort except that the
glazing needs lower energy transmittance and higher visible transmit-
tances. There are some similarities between the results of Carlucci et al.
(2015) and the presented work. For example, night ventilation is im-
portant to achieve thermal comfort in summer; higher visual trans-
mittance is required for visual comfort; overhang projection has high
impacts on visual and thermal comfort. The main diﬀerence is the re-
quirement of thermal insulation of building envelopes: the results of
Carlucci et al. (2015) suggested highly insulated building envelope,
while the results of the presented work suggests low thermal insulation.
This diﬀerence is mainly because of the diﬀerent climates considered.

apartment buildings shows that with solar panels covering the roof and
partial exterior walls, the energy demand of up to 19 ﬂoors can be met.
Sensitivity analysis shows that overhang projection, window types
and WWR are the top three most inﬂuential variables for daylighting of
both a representative landed house and apartment building. The top
three most inﬂuential variables for thermal comfort of landed house are
window status, roof solar absorptance and WWR. The top three most
inﬂuential variables for thermal comfort of apartment building are
window status, WWR and overhang projection. Light eﬃciency is the
most inﬂuential variable for energy eﬃciency and cost eﬀectiveness of
both building types. For energy eﬃciency and cost eﬀectiveness of
landed house, water heater eﬃciency and air-conditioner operating rate
are the second and the third inﬂuential variables, respectively. For
energy eﬃciency and cost eﬀectiveness of apartment building, air-
conditioner COP and water heater eﬃciency are the second and the
third inﬂuential variables, respectively.
Further discussions on the results of the case study show that al-
though some variables need to be carefully selected to balance day-
lighting and natural cooling, the two objectives do not always contra-
dict each other regarding certain variables, such as WWR. Similarly,
environmental aims and economic aims do not always contradict each
other on certain variables. In fact, more energy eﬃcient appliances tend
to result in lower LCC. The social aims do not contradict the environ-
mental and the economic aims, as the ﬁndings show that designing for
daylighting and natural cooling contributes to the improvement of
energy eﬃciency and cost eﬀectiveness. Comparisons between the
proposed work and some related work show that the proposed method
is a more holistic approach, which aims to address all the TBL factors.
Diﬀerences are found between the results of this case study (based in a
tropical climate) and results from studies based in non-tropical cli-

4.4. Limitations

The limitations of this study are listed below.

• The case study is based on the predetermined building shapes and
layouts. The building performance (including natural cooling, day-
lighting, energy and LCC) and solar energy potential should vary as
the shape and layout of the building changes. For example, the
apartment building in this study is H-shaped. For other building
shapes (e.g. L-shaped, star- shaped, square or rectangular), the op-
timization

and

would
be
diﬀerent.
Therefore, further studies may take building shapes and layouts into
consideration to determine the best building performances and
maximum solar energy potentials.
• The energy consumption of the apartment building considered in
the case study consists energy consumption of all households in the
building, while other building facilities, such as corridor lights and
lifts, are not included in this analysis.
• Shades from nearby blocks are not considered in the analysis. Plot
ratio may be considered as a variable for future work.
• Discount rate and energy price escalation rate are not analyzed in
this study. Sensitivity analysis may be conducted to explore the ef-
fect of the above factors on the outcome of the optimization.
• In practice, there may be certain limitations on the range of vari-
ables. For instance, the orientation of the building may be limited by
roads and the available site area. However, the proposed method is
still applicable while the variable types and ranges vary.
• Cost of renewable energy system is not considered in this study. The
presented work aims to ﬁnd the minimum requirement of RES to
achieve net-zero energy. For future work, the cost eﬀectiveness of
renewable energy system may be analyzed. Along with the advances
of the RES industry, renewable energy systems (e.g. solar photo-
voltaic system) are believed to become more and more cost eﬀec-
tive.
• The case study is based on buildings in Singapore. For locations
which have diﬀerent building types and/or climates, the optimiza-
tion results should be diﬀerent.
• The optimization algorithm applied in this study is selected based on
the most used algorithms in the literature. However, there are many
optimization algorithms available. Comparisons between diﬀerent
optimization approaches can be found in Christoph, Thomas, Ralph,
and Jan (2018) (single-objective optimization) and Hamdy, Nguyen,
and Hensen (2016) (multi-objective optimization).
