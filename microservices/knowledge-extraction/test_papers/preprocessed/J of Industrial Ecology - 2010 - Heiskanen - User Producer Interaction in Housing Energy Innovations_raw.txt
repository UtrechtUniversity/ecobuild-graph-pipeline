Von Hippel and colleagues have highlighted the crucial role of
users in innovation in different industries and types of prod-
ucts. They describe the innovation process in terms of the
distinct domains of knowledge that producers and users pos-
sess. Producers have knowledge about technical solutions and
users about their needs, the context of use, and their own
capabilities as users. Both sets of knowledge are characterized
by “stickiness”: They move relatively freely within their own
domain but are difﬁcult to transfer outside of it.
In the case of radical innovations for sustainable consump-
tion, the problem of “sticky information” is compounded. Both
producers and consumers need to reach out of their conven-
tional competencies and search for new solutions. “Societal
actors,” such as government bodies or environmental experts,
can show the way to such solutions, but this new knowledge
needs to be integrated with the “sticky” knowledge about
everyday practices in production and consumption.
In the present article we attempt to conceptualize the role
and interaction of user and producer knowledge with the
knowledge of environmental experts in housing energy inno-
vations. We do so by applying the user−producer interaction
framework to a case study on the introduction of low-energy
housing concepts in Finland. On the basis of this analysis, we
draw conclusions on the potential and limitations of today’s
practices in the ﬁeld. For example, we suggest that user in-
volvement can help to enhance the acceptance of low-energy
solutions but that the methods for involving users need to be
adapted to the particular circumstances in each industry.

communication gap is also a reality, and it would
exist even if prices and legislation more force-

energy efﬁciency.
In recent years, the main driver for introduc-
ing low-energy buildings in Finland has been so-
ciety, not users or producers of houses. Various
governmental bodies, such as public research in-
stitutes and energy advisors, have tried to ﬁll the
communication gap by information-based volun-
tary measures. The idea of using mainly volun-
tary measures has been based on the argument
that there are still so many competing low-energy
housing concepts that it is better that produc-
ers and users of houses—and not the regulator—
decide which technological solutions are intro-
duced in the market.
In the remainder of this article, we outline
the communication problem ﬁrst on a general
level. Then we make an in-depth exploration of
the communication gap by analyzing the evo-
lution and experiences of a Finnish program to
promote low-energy housing. Finally, we suggest
some solutions to address the communication
challenges.

Introduction: Why Is There No
Progress in Energy Efﬁciency
Even Though the Technology Is
Within Reach?

According to the European Commission (EC)
Green Paper on Energy Efﬁciency (EC 2006), the
buildings sector accounts for 40% of Europe’s en-
ergy requirements. Researchers have argued that
more than one-ﬁfth of the present energy con-
sumption could be saved by 2010 if more am-
bitious standards were applied to new buildings
and to existing ones being refurbished. Energy
costs are also becoming increasingly signiﬁcant
for households, especially in cold climates, as the
prices of electricity and oil are rising.
This is the situation in Finland as well: “There
are market-ready technologies in Finland that
could cut the energy bills in buildings by 20%
by 2015,” said Senior Researcher Pekka Huovila
at the publication event for Energy Use, published
by VTT Technical Research Centre of Finland
last May (P¨oys¨a 2007).
Energy conservation is possible and neces-
sary, but why is so little actually happening? A
well-known columnist (Merikallio 2007, 34) for
Suomen Kuvalehti, a popular Finnish weekly mag-
azine, complained about the situation in a column
titled “Who Will Cure My Oil Addiction?”:

Every time I hear a rumble from the boiler
room, I get annoyed. I am burning some more
oil again, and contributing some more to cli-
mate change. . . . The small everyday deci-
sions are easy: you can leave the car at home,
turn out the lights and recycle everything that
you possibly can. But when it is time to over-
haul your heating system in an old house, you
step into the “twilight zone.” The Internet
abounds with geothermal heat pumps, pellets,
district heat, air heat pumps, solar panels, oil-
solar combinations. Every salesman says their
offer is fantastic, but no one tells you what the
pitfalls are. . . . Even making a bid for tenders
is so complicated that you give up. . . . And
all the heating systems are either expensive or
extremely expensive. When will they ﬁnally
set up a proper advisory system? Couldn’t an
energy expert come to your home with all the
meters and calculators and tell you what the
best heating system is for you? I promise to
offer them coffee and cake!

The problem of sticky information is further
complicated in the case of societal innovations,
such as energy efﬁciency (Kivisaari et al. 2004).
Here, societal actors, such as public energy agen-
cies, have their own perspective on the innova-
tion, which differs from the perspectives of the
users and producers. The societal actor thus must
try to communicate with both producers and users
to promote its own knowledge and understand
the requirements and competencies of the mar-
ket actors (see ﬁgure 1b).
Designers and societal actors do have certain
representations of users in mind when construct-
ing products, yet they usually fail to anticipate
the practices of usage that eventually develop as
the products are used and integrated into daily
life (Akrich 1995). Moreover, when technolo-
gies are in use, they are often misused or used
in ways that are different than was anticipated
(Rohracher 2003, 2005). A frequently proposed
solution to this problem is intensiﬁed interaction
between the world of designers and the world of
users. This can consist of designer participation
in the user context, user participation in design,
or user innovation.

same kind of knowledge as experts do, they know
nothing. Other authors consider the problem of
energy knowledge from the opposite perspective
(Guy and Shove 1998; Shove 1998). Experts sim-
ply frame energy use in different terms—often
ones that are distant from ordinary households’
concerns. They fail to understand why house-
holds behave “irrationally” because they fail to
grasp the logic of energy use (e.g., Parnell and
Popovic-Larsen 2005).
The exchange of energy-efﬁciency knowledge
among experts and laypeople reﬂects a fundamen-
tal problem in product innovation. Von Hippel
(1994, 1998) has termed this a problem of “sticky
information”: Information about users’ needs and
manufacturers’ capabilities is highly contextual,
tacit, and difﬁcult to transfer from one site to an-
other (Von Hippel 2005). This problem slows
down the product innovation process—many
rounds of information exchange are needed to
establish facts and clarify perspectives (ﬁgure 1a).

Designer Participation in the User
Context

One of the ways designers can improve their
user representations is to make more detailed
studies of usage practices, such as via ﬁeld stud-
ies or detailed analyses of the historical devel-
opment of usage patterns. A number of design
tools have also been developed on the basis of
ethnographic or ﬁeld observations (e.g., Leonard
1995). We are not aware of direct applications
of ethnography-based design tools in the ﬁeld of
sustainable innovation. But, for example, Chap-
pels and Shove (2005) have made extensive use
of ﬁeld research to investigate and problematize
the concept of “thermal comfort,” and Quitzau
and Røpke (2008) have analyzed the drivers of
resource-consuming bathroom practices.

The MotiVoittaja Project:
Mainstreaming Low-Energy
Housing

As a case study on the role of user knowl-
edge in the introduction of energy innovations,
we consider a recent Finnish project aimed at
promoting low-energy housing. In 1999, Mo-
tiva, a state-owned company responsible for pro-
moting energy efﬁciency and renewable energy,
launched a project to mainstream and “normal-
ize” the concept of low-energy housing through
a technology procurement competition and la-
beling system (called MotiVoittaja) targeted at
the producers and users of prefabricated de-
tached homes (more details are found in the
work by Halme et al. [2005] and Heiskanen et al.
[2007]).
Low-energy housing as conceptualized in the
project refers to a set of technologies, includ-
ing increased thermal insulation, low-energy win-
dows, reduced air leakages, recovery of heat from
exhaust air, extraction of energy with heat pumps,
passive solar energy, and building orientation. A
key aspect is a systems view of designing a house.
Design is based on a thorough understanding,
control, and utilization of the energy ﬂows within
a building.
The MotiVoittaja project sought to promote
the diffusion of the low-energy concept through a
technology procurement competition. Technol-
ogy procurement is an instrument for stimulating
innovation through a targeted acquisition pro-
cess. An inﬂuential buyer or group of buyers for-
mulates the requirements, and market transfor-
mation is further inﬂuenced by support activities.
In the MotiVoittaja project, the award was de-
signed to function as a label of endorsement,
allowing prospective users to identify certiﬁed

User Participation

Another potential solution is to involve users
in the design process to gain their comments
about designers’ ideas. For example, Rohracher’s
(2003) group interviews of the prospective users
of smart-home technologies and balanced ven-
tilation systems revealed many doubts that users
had about how these technologies would ﬁt into
their everyday life (see also Spaargaren et al.
2006).

User Innovation

Users’ own inventions and innovations can
be a direct source of commercial products. Von
Hippel and colleagues (Von Hippel 2005; Jeppe-
sen and Molin 2003) have found that, at least in
some product groups, a large proportion of users
invent and customize their own products. For ex-
ample, user innovation has traditionally had a
signiﬁcant role in alternative energy technology
(Jamison 2001). As contemporary examples, Or-
netzeder and Rohracher (2006) have analyzed
two Austrian cases of user innovation in solar
collectors and wood chip boilers. For our pur-
pose, it is reasonable to make a distinction be-
tween two types of user innovation. The ﬁrst type
includes those cases in which a user customizes
the product according to his or her needs and
local requirements without inventing anything

Manufacturers of prefabricated houses were se-
lected as the target group because of their growing
market share. According to the current estimate,
their share of the detached house market in Fin-
land was about 66% in 2005, compared to 44% in
1995 (PTT 2009). In 2003, there were about 40
producers on the market, producing about 10,000
houses annually. The industry was fragmented
among a great many small players, with annual
turnovers ranging from less than 2 million to 46
million euros1 (PTT 2003).
The most popular method for setting up a pre-
fabricated house is for users to do part of the
work themselves or have it done by contractors,
but turnkey constructions are also gaining pop-
ularity (Halme et al. 2005). In the ﬁrst case we
call users homebuilders, and in the second case
we call them buyers of houses. We assume that
homebuilders are interested in customizing their
house, and by doing so and maybe also doing
part of the work themselves, they may make user-
speciﬁc or more general user innovations. Buyers
are expected to be satisﬁed with the producers’
solutions, but they also can be innovative by be-
ing ready to accept solutions that are new to the
market.
Typically, when a detached house is built, the
problem of sticky information is based on a num-
ber of factors, which all are related to the fact that
a detached house is a complex product, more or
less customized according to users’ needs and lo-
cal requirements, and is often made by a project-
based ﬁrm with many subcontractors. Gann and
Salter (2000) and Taylor and Levitt (2005) ar-
gue that in these circumstances communication
problems are typical and innovations are difﬁcult
to achieve. The large number of actors (producer,
subcontractors, user, and local regulator) as such
contributes to high stickiness, because there is
huge need for communication and trust among
different actors. The homebuilders’ eagerness to
customize their house and ﬁnd new personal el-
ements is often greater than their actual capa-
bilities when they start their ﬁrst home-building
processes. In addition, the manufacturers of pre-
fabricated houses are not oriented to collect in-
formation on customers’ various needs.

The MotiVoittaja project tried to address the
above issues of high stickiness mainly by orga-
nizing the project in such a way that users were
present at various stages of the project to a greater
extent than in the ordinary case. The forms and
intensity of user participation are described brieﬂy
in the following sections.

Assembling the Initial Purchaser Group

Gaining market commitment from initial pur-
chasers is an important part of technology pro-
curement projects. In this case, purchasers were
private consumers, so assembling the group was
difﬁcult. Finally, a buyer group of 40 families
was gathered. Yet the buyers turned out to be
quite reluctant to make binding contracts with
the manufacturers. Some were not satisﬁed with
the standard awarded models and requested so
many modiﬁcations that the houses no longer
met the energy-efﬁciency criteria. The most im-
portant initial buyers were purchasers of Hous-
ing Fair houses. Three awarded models were con-
structed for the Housing Fair and sold before the
fair. The families who moved into these houses
became the most important showcases for the
MotiVoittaja concept.

Planning, Setting Criteria, and Selecting
the Winning Entries

The planning, criteria-setting, and selection
of winning entries were done by experts: the
project managers, the expert evaluators, and the
jury involved in the competition. There were,
however, two user representatives in the jury.
In particular, the jury member from the Na-
tional Association for Detached Housing Con-
struction represented the homebuilder’s perspec-
tive in the project. Moreover, the project man-
agers and the jury made an effort to address some
of the users’ (perceived) concerns related to low-
energy housing—namely costs, convenience, and
indoor air quality.

Informal Communications

In the formal communications, the media cov-
erage was quite favorable. The unofﬁcial commu-
nication that was ongoing on Internet discussion
forums revealed a more mixed reception. Opin-
ions diverged on what low-energy housing means,
and much debate took place about real-life heat-
ing costs. One recurring topic was whether to in-
vest in geothermal heat pumps or increased insu-
lation. This discussion implies that the “systemic
design” idea was not adopted by all users. There
was also some competition between contrasting
concepts of ecological housing: one based on tra-
ditional construction methods and natural mate-
rials, and the other a “modern” concept involv-
ing well-sealed structures and highly controlled
air and energy ﬂows.

Formal Communications

Most of the communication on the project fol-
lowed a top-down model. Communications were
directed at the trade and daily press and other
media. The project received extensive media cov-
erage and favorable press. The project managers
themselves, however, were of the opinion that
more efforts should have been made in commu-
nications after the competition.
Nonetheless, one important communication
success was achieved through participation in the
Finnish nationwide Housing Fair events. These
annual events create a new model housing de-
velopment each year in a different location and
illustrate novel technologies through demonstra-
tion constructions. After the event, the houses
are sold to “typical” families, and the fairgrounds
are turned into a residential area. MotiVoittaja
was presented as a concept at the 2001 event,
and demonstration houses were built for the 2002
event.

energy housing, the project attempted to align

users, especially the growing group of middle-
class buyers who rely on ready-made market of-
ferings. Yet users are a varied group. They typi-
cally consider a number of heating and construc-

User innovation implies a deeper and more fun-
damental participation by users. Innovative users
actually come up with solutions that may later be
integrated into commercial-scale designs. User
innovations are not uncommon in low-energy
housing; in fact, many early models were built
by individual lead users for themselves (see also
Lovell 2007). Such concepts are quite varie-
gated, ranging from high-tech solutions with a
high input by contractors to quite low-tech self-
construction solutions. They are usually closely
tailored to individual users’ needs (Daniels 2007).
The notion of user innovation, especially a
general type of user innovation, is partly contra-
dictory to the vision of the MotiVoittaja project,
which aimed to go beyond individual custom-
tailored solutions and to move low-energy hous-
ing into the mainstream market. We agree that
it is not obvious that even the best users’ self-
designs or user-speciﬁc innovations are readily
transferable to the mainstream market. Never-
theless, homebuilders accumulate a lot of use-
ful experience during their homebuilding project,
and the Internet discussion sites show that these
homebuilders are willing to share their knowl-
edge and ideas with others. The Internet might
also offer a solution for systematic pooling of user
knowledge. For example, Halme and colleagues
(2005) suggested that the MotiVoittaja project
might have considered an “open design platform”
rather than encouraging each manufacturer to
come up with its own competition entry. Alter-
natively, the project could have encouraged man-
ufacturers to equip homebuilders with appropri-
ate tool kits for user-speciﬁc innovations that do
not change the core structure of the house as a
low-energy solution.
We also argue that engaging user-speciﬁc in-
novation most likely also would have helped the
project to communicate the rationale underlying

Approaches to Intensifying
Knowledge Exchange Among
Users and Experts

We have seen in the previous case that Mo-
tiVoittaja experienced some of the classical prob-
lems in engaging users (Rohracher 2003): The
user representations were too inﬂexible and failed
to anticipate unexpected ways of using the prod-
uct (i.e., tailoring the prefabricated house). Next
we consider whether more interaction among
users, producers, and societal actors might have
helped to avoid some of the failures. We revisit
the three categories of user involvement pre-
sented earlier and consider the contribution that
they could have made to the project.
Designer participation in the user context refers
to close interpretive research of the user context
by the designers. In the MotiVoittaja project, it
seems that a better knowledge of the user context
mostly likely would have helped the project to
better address usability, utility, acceptance, and
user context issues. In fact, the designers of the
project were, to some extent, aware of the diver-
sity of the user base and the complexity of the
homebuilding process. More systematic ﬁeld ob-
servations might have reinforced the role of this
knowledge in the design of the project. Akrich
(1995) has identiﬁed this problem: Even when
useful experiential knowledge or market research
is available, designers’ user representations tend
to converge around predeﬁned project targets.
User participation in design refers to the direct
involvement of users in the design of the de-
velopment and promotion of the innovation. In
the MotiVoittaja project, such approaches (e.g.,
user groups, focus groups) might have revealed, in
particular, the reservations that users had vis-`a-
vis their role. Issues that might have come to the
surface include the lack of trust in prefabricated
housing manufacturers’ claims and the users’ de-
sire to customize their home. Moreover, a closer
understanding of how users understand energy use

Conclusions

The MotiVoittaja project was quite ambitious
and user friendly when compared with many
other energy-efﬁciency projects (see, e.g., Vreuls
2006). Yet we have seen that even this state-of-
the art project most likely could have been im-
proved had users been involved more intensively
and had the project also made use of user partic-
ipation in communication about the project.

1. Methods for user participation need to
be adapted to the needs of speciﬁc user−
producer (and societal actor) constella-
tions. The problem of sticky information
varies between industries (see, e.g., Ogawa
1998; Gann and Salter 2000; Aoyama &
Izushi 2008). The example of homebuild-
ing is a special case: Homebuilders have a
strong involvement in their homebuilding
projects, and they have a lot of ideas about
their needs and expectations for their new
home. Nonetheless, most homebuilders
are ﬁrst-time users, with little knowledge
of the homebuilding process.
2. Knowledge and experience are differen-
tially distributed within the user commu-
nity (Von Hippel 2005). Homebuilders
acquire a lot of information as they go
through the homebuilding process. Expe-
rienced users are thus much more knowl-
edgeable than novice users. Even today,
users share much of their accumulated
knowledge with each other, and promot-
ing such peer-to-peer knowledge sharing

All in all, we argue that the communication
gap between producers and users can, by and
large, be ﬁlled by the introduction of new commu-
nication skills and methods, as indicated above.
Improved communication may be the best way to
produce novel innovations in the housing sector.
There is an urgent need, however, to improve
the energy efﬁciency in housing during the com-
ing decade. If a communication-based voluntary
strategy is not strong enough, there obviously is a
good reason to move toward more stringent direct
regulation.
