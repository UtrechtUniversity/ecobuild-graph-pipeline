Loss of natural habitats and increased human activity results in warming up of cities and a reduced biodiversity.
Vertical greenery systems (VGSs) have been proposed to mitigate rising temperatures and the loss of biodiversity.
So far, studies on existing VGSs have only analysed a single ecosystem service and their performance has not
been compared to that of their natural counterparts. Here, air and surface temperature measurements as well as
biodiversity observations were conducted on two different types of existing VGSs (climber, foliage) on buildings
in Singapore and compared to non-vegetated building walls and vegetated natural cliffs. Our results show that
VGSs act as temperature buffer creating a cooling layer towards the building wall during the day and a warming
layer during the night. Furthermore, VGSs host significantly more animals than non-vegetated walls, however,
VGS have less animals than vegetated natural cliffs. Moreover, a positive correlation was found between the
amount of surrounding vegetation (green cover, tree density within 10 m of VGS), thickness of vegetation, plant
richness on VGS and biodiversity observed on VGSs. Together, VGSs not only contribute with their aesthetics but
can also be used to regulate daytime temperature in a tropical city like Singapore and to mitigate, at least in part,
the loss of habitat for biodiversity.

Our cities are warming up due to loss of natural habitats and
increased human activity caused by population growth and exacerbated
by climate change [1]. Urbanization results in extensive low-albedo
infrastructure like buildings and roads, which reinforce the urban heat
island (UHI) effect [2]. For instance, a comparative study among 419
global big cities showed a surface UHI intensity between 0.4 and 3 ◦C
[3]. In Singapore, increased temperatures of up to 4.2 ◦C were measured
in the city center compared to surrounding rural areas [4]. The loss of
natural habitats and increased human activity also results in reduced
biodiversity, air quality, aesthetics and health of citizens [5,6]. Pro-
jections suggest that habitat has been lost for at least one-third of urban
vertebrate biodiversity due to urban land expansion [7]. Globally, land
use changes have already caused loss of local species richness by about
77 % and loss of total abundance by about 40 % [8].

Relative humidity (RH) was measured using a hand-held Center 315
temperature and humidity meter at a distance of 0.2 m from the VGS at
1.6 m from the ground (Fig. S2). Two handheld RH readings were taken
during every data collection bout – before and after biodiversity data
collection (see Section 2.2).
Air temperature was measured using three high-resolution thermo-
chron iButton® DS1921H sensors (Maxim Integrated Products, San Jose,
CA, USA) that recorded air temperature every five minutes for four
consecutive days to document the microclimate at the air exposed side of
and behind the VGSs as well as around the control walls [22,23]. Using a
stationary approach, the sensors were attached at 1.8 m height from the
ground at the air-exposed side within the vegetation of the VGS, behind
the VGS on the building wall, and to the respective non-vegetated
control wall (Fig. S2). Air temperature data collection overlapped with
the biodiversity data collection period.

2.2. Biodiversity data collection

Active visual surveys were conducted as area counts on the walls to
systematically monitor animal diversity [21]. The surveyor actively
scanned and searched the vertical surface of each of the 20 walls for 20
min during each visit counting the number of different animal species as
well as their abundance. Each wall was visited a total of six times as
described in Section 2.2 however per day only a maximum of two
measurements were conducted at the same wall. Sunset typically hap-
pens close to or after 7:00pm in Singapore. The skies are typically bright
till 7:30pm. Therefore, biodiversity measurements were typically con-
ducted in the morning (between 7.00am and 11.00am), late morning /
early afternoon (between 11.00am and 3.00pm), and / or late afternoon
/ early evening (between 3.00pm and 7.00pm) during a period of six
months. Measurements were spread throughout the day to capture times
when different animals were active e.g., birds in the early morning and
insects in late morning and afternoon hours. Measurements were only
done during conditions of no rain.
Only individuals interacting with the wall were considered; i.e. those
that were foraging, resting, seeking shelter or nesting on the wall. Ani-
mals that were observed around but not interacting with the VGS were
excluded from the analysis. Binoculars and cameras were used to iden-
tify and document the animals. The observed animals were identified to
the species level where possible. Where this was not possible, animals
were identified to the lowest taxonomic level possible (genus, order or
family). Junior team members were trained and supervised by the last
author, who has a formal training and over 15 years of expertise in
studying tropical biodiversity. The plant species composition of each of
the 12 walls with vegetation was obtained through a botanical census
with experts and existing planting lists provided by building owners and
park managers (Table S1). Vegetation thickness of each VGS was
measured from the VGS structure to the leaf or branch growing furthest
towards the air-exposed side of the VGS using a measurement tape.

2.4. Land use land cover

Land use land cover (LULC) was measured for the green area within
the 50 m, 100 m, 500 m and 1000 m radius from the walls. Green areas
are defined as vegetated surfaces [24]. The analyzed distances from the
wall were chosen as a gradient of minimal distances for suitable
ecological corridors as habitats in urban environments [25]. ArcGIS Pro
ESRI 3.1.1 combined with a publicly accessible high-resolution map of
Singapore’s terrestrial ecosystems with data from 2003 to 2018 and
processed in 2019 [26] were used to determine the green areas around
the walls and to calculate the LULC. The percentage of green areas
within each radius band as well as the portion of green area contributed
by the respective VGS were tabulated (Table 2). Furthermore, the
number of trees was counted within 10 m from the studied walls
(Table 2).

2.5. Canopy coverage

Canopy coverage was measured at 1 m from the ground and 0.5 m
from the walls towards four cardinal directions in a 90-degree angle
from the wall’s cardinal direction using a spherical densiometer (Lem-
mon Forest Densiometer®) [27]. Canopy openings were counted for four
equi-spaced dots in each of the 24 squares in the grid. The total amount
of empty dots per direction were multiplied by 1.04 to obtain the per-
centage of overhead area not occupied by canopy averaging the four
directions.

3.2. Surface temperature

Average daily air temperature in front of the walls (hand-held probe)
was not significantly different within and between wall categories (p ≥
0.221). Also, the average daily air temperature in front of the walls was
not significantly different from that at the air-exposed side of the walls
within and between wall categories (p ≥0.196). By contrast, the average
daily air temperature at the air-exposed side of the VGSs was on average
0.7 ◦C and 0.6 ◦C higher than behind the foliage and climber VGSs,
respectively (p = 0.002 in both cases). Specifically, the air temperature
at the air-exposed side of the foliage VGSs was significantly higher than
behind this VGS type in the early morning (p < 0.001) (Fig. 2A). By
contrast, air temperature at the air-exposed side of the climber VGSs was
significantly lower than behind this VGS type in the early morning (p =
0.015) (Fig. 2B). From the late morning until the late afternoon / early
evening, air temperature was significantly lower behind both the foliage
and the climber VGSs (p < 0.001 in both cases) compared to the air
temperature at the air-exposed side of the VGSs showing a cooling effect
of the VGSs during the late morning and afternoon hours (Fig. 2C–F).
Compared to the early morning, later morning / early afternoon and late
afternoon / early evening, the average air temperature significantly
dropped during the night by 1.4 ◦C, 2.2 ◦C and 2.2 ◦C on foliage and
climber control walls, respectively, and by 1.5 ◦C, 3.5 ◦C, and 2.2 ◦C at
the air-exposed side of the foliage and climber VGSs and the cliffs,
respectively (p < 0.001 in both cases) . In the night, both the foliage and
climber VGSs reduced cooling of the wall keeping the air behind the
vegetation warmer than at its surface (p < 0.001 in both cases) (Fig. 2G
and H).

3.3. Biodiversity on VGSs

A total of 280 animal species were observed interacting with the
walls, of which 65 % (n = 182) could be identified to the species, genus
or family level and 35 % (n = 98) at the order level or labeled as un-
identified (Tables 3 and S2). Insects made up 75 % of the species
observed, while the remaining 25 % consisted of arachnids (11 %), snails
(6 %), birds (5 %) and millipedes, reptiles, amphibians, crustaceans and
mammals (4 %) (Fig. 5). A total of 59 % (n = 107) out of the 182 animals
that were identified to the species, genus or family level were native to
Singapore and 10 % (n = 19) were non-native and 31 % (n = 56) had a
status of unknown origin (Table S2). Most native species were found on
natural cliffs (n = 60), followed by climber (n = 40) and foliage (n = 39)
VGSs. Foliage and climber control walls had the least number of native
species (n = 12 and n = 7, respectively). The largest number of non-
native species was found on foliage VGSs (n = 9), followed by climber
VGSs (n = 6), foliage control VGSs (n = 4), and natural cliffs (n = 3) and
climber control walls (n = 0).

while the ant species Pheidole megacephala was observed on 13 walls

(n = 1046) and 86 % (n = 4575) of all counted individuals were ants
(Formicidae), respectively, while these numbers were 64 % (n = 3062)
and 83 % (n = 2106) for natural cliffs and control walls.
Since ants live and work in colonies it is very probable that all
observed individuals on VGS belonged to the same ant colony [28]. Also,
a few ant species dominated the animal population, and therefore, an-
imals with n > 300 individuals (i.e. the ants described above per site)
were removed from the data set to assess their impact on the statistical
analysis. In this case, a significantly higher animal abundance was found
on natural cliffs (n = 1101) compared to foliage and climber control
walls (n = 197 and n = 227, respectively) (p = 0.047, p = 0.004, p =
0.005) (Fig. 6C). Animal abundance was not significantly different be-
tween natural cliffs (n = 1101) and climber (n = 681) and foliage (n =
468) VGSs (p = 0.279 and p = 0.865, respectively), as well as between
climber (n = 681) and foliage (n = 468) VGSs (p = 0.832) (Fig. 6C).
Moreover, no significant difference in animal abundance was found
between foliage and climber VGSs and their respective control walls (p =
0.681 and p = 0.225, respectively) (Fig. 6C).
The Shannon-Weiner diversity values were not significantly different
between climber and foliage VGSs, natural cliffs and control walls (p =
0.348, H = 0.23–2.80) (Fig. 7A). By taking out the animals with > 300
individuals per site, the Shannon-Weiner index was significantly higher
on natural cliffs compared to climber and foliage control walls (p =
0.004, p = 0.020, respectively) as well as on climber VGSs compared to
their control walls (p = 0.016) (Fig. 7B). Yet, there was no significant
difference between the foliage VGSs and their control walls (p = 0.104)
as well as between foliage and climber VGSs (p = 0.998). The highest
Shannon-Weiner index was found for natural cliffs with an average of H

= 2.79, decreasing to H = 2.55 and H = 2.48 for climber and foliage
VGSs and to H = 1.29 and H = 1.56 for control walls of climber and
foliage VGSs, respectively.

3.4. Influence of temperature on animal diversity on VGSs

The average daily air temperature at the air-exposed side of the VGSs
and in front of the control walls was not correlated with the observed
number of different animal species, animal abundance and the Shannon-
Weiner index (with or without animals > 300 individuals per wall) (r2 ≤
0.59, p ≥0.175) (Table S4). By contrast, daily average surface temper-
ature on the lower unit of the walls did correlate with animal abundance
(r2 = 0.56, p = 0.011) (Fig. 8A, Table S4). Animal abundance signifi-
cantly decreased with an increase of air temperature at the air-exposed
side of the VGSs and surface temperature (Table S4).

3.5. Influence of wall characteristics on animal diversity

Wall area (Table 1) had no significant influence on animal species,
animal abundance and Shannon-Weiner index (with all different ani-
mals included; r2 ≤0.16, p ≥0.37) (Table S4). By contrast, vegetation
thickness (Table 1) was positively correlated with the number of animal
species and animal abundance (with all animals included) as well as the
Shannon-Weiner index without animals with > 300 individuals (r2 ≥
0.58, p ≤0.008) (Fig. 8D–F, Table S4). Vegetation thickness was not
correlated with the Shannon-Weiner index with all different animals (r2

= 0.25, p = 0.284) (Table S4). A strong positive correlation was
observed between plant richness and the number of animal species on
the walls (including all different animals) as well as the respective
Shannon-Weiner index with all animals and without animals with > 300
individuals (r2 ≥0.46, p ≤0.041) (Fig. 8G–I, Table S4).

4.1. Temperature performance of VGSs

Air temperature and relative humidity of VGSs and cliffs were similar
to that of the control walls, indicating that our selected study sites were
located in similar microclimatic conditions setting the context for a valid
comparison analysis between our study sites. Notably, the average daily
air temperature at the VGSs was on average 0.6–0.7 ◦C higher than
behind the VGSs. Thus, these VGSs were cooling the walls of the building
during the day. By contrast, the foliage and climber VGSs reduced
cooling of the walls during the night, keeping the air behind the vege-
tation warmer than at their surface. These results confirm the hypothesis
that vegetated walls provide temperature control of buildings in a
tropical region and are in line with other studies on urban VGSs in other
regions in the world [29–31]. Although the albedo of vegetation is
generally lower than that of white or light grey surfaces like building
walls, evaporative cooling could be the determining factor for the
measured lower surface temperatures on VGSs and natural cliffs [32]. A
significantly lower air temperature was measured throughout the day
behind foliage VGSs compared to that behind the climber VGSs and
within the vegetation of the cliffs. This may be (partly) due to the ver-
tical irrigation system that drips water at different wall heights into the
planter pots enhancing evaporation and thereby causing increased
adiabatic cooling [33]. By contrast, watering of climber VGSs happened
solely through the soil on the ground level resulting in a reduced water

4. Discussion

In this study we quantified the ecosystem services of temperature
regulation and habitat provisioning on existing foliage and climber VGSs
in Singapore and compared these results with natural cliffs and non-
vegetated walls. Comparing the ecological performance of building
VGSs to their natural counterparts is a novel approach to evaluate if
building walls perform to their full potential in providing ecosystem
services [18]. Overall, we found that cliffs were more performative with
respect to animal diversity (including native species) and can thus be
used as a biomimetic model to improve animal biodiversity on VGSs.
Furthermore, we found that lower air and surface temperature at the
air-exposed side of the building walls and VGSs correlates with a higher
animal abundance however not with more diversity.

that found that a targeted selection of native plants on the VGS is more
important than having a higher number of non-targeted plant species to
attract native animals [44]. As a conclusion, VGSs can effectively sup-
port urban biodiversity and ecological conservation of habitat for native
species [45,46]. An implication of building VGSs for the facility man-
agement is a potentially increased maintenance related to regular
pruning and monitoring related to watering and temperature stress,
sunlight exposure, nutrition, disease and pests [47].

Together, our results indicate that vegetation positively impacts
animal diversity. No statistical difference was found between the animal
species richness on the cliffs, and the foliage and climber VGSs but their
number was significantly higher than those on the control walls.
Shannon-Weiner index was highest for natural cliffs (H = 2.79) followed
by climber and foliage VGSs (H = 2.55 and H = 2.48) and the control
walls (H = 1.29 and H = 1.56) when species with > 300 individuals per
site were excluded. The majority of animals observed on all wall types
were insects, which can be explained (in part) by the fact that vertical
surfaces such as VGSs seem to be most suitable for flying and climbing
animals such as insects, spiders, and for some birds [36]. Ant species
were the most abundant animals on the wall types representing 56% - 86
% of all counted animals. A greater number of insect diversity in urban
spaces can help support plant regeneration by offering crucial pollina-
tion services as well as provide food for higher tropic animals (e.g. birds)
thereby improving resilience across food webs. A larger herbivorous
insect diversity usually also attracts more predators, such as insectivo-
rous birds, which can in turn regulate insect populations [37]. Apart
from the benefits (which also include aesthetics and thermal comfort),
some of these insects on VGSs might lead to an increased risk of pests or
pathogens that can negatively affect plant and animal and in some cases
also human health [38–40]. More insects on buildings may also pose a
challenge for building maintenance and facility managers as many ur-
banites worldwide have the perception of associating insects with pests
[41,42].

4.4. Biodiversity and presence of trees

Our results indicate that the presence of trees within a 10 m radius
around the VGS positively impacts animal diversity. Surrounding trees
provide stepping stone habitats specifically for flying but also for
climbing or crawling insects and other animals [48,49]. More research is
required to collect detailed data on the adequate plant species and dis-
tances of vegetation and trees from the VGSs to create habitat connec-
tivity and maintain or improve the positive effect on animal diversity.
Habitat disturbance generally inhibits animals to safely migrate even
when habitats are within travelable distance from each other [50]. The
size and quality, including heterogeneity, of urban ecological connectors
can be a strong influencer on the amount and type of biodiversity that
we found on the investigated VGSs and should be taken into account
when planning VGS and on-ground green habitats [51].
The higher number of total animal species as well as native animal
species on natural cliffs despite it having less plant species points to-
wards a better performance of natural cliffs to provide habitat to a larger
diversity of animal species compared to foliage and climber VGSs on
buildings. Therefore, natural cliffs can serve as a biomimetic model for
design of VGSs to optimize that habitat for native species. Natural cliffs
provide habitat to a higher number of different animals potentially due
to their proximity to blue and green areas, the larger number of trees
within 10 m distance to the cliff [52,53] and the cliff’s structural
complexity. The tree species, height and canopy size within 10 m dis-
tance to the walls was not identified in this study. However, large trees –
as often found in parks - and native vegetation can serve as stepping
stones for particular bird, insect or other animals [54]. By having a more
complex structure for VGS with stratification allow for the establishing
of microclimate and ecological niches. Planting trees near VGS can also

could be achieved by selecting plants which grow thicker or by adapting
the maintenance regime with lower trimming frequency. Also, a positive
correlation was found between plant richness and animal species rich-

4.5. Biodiversity and surrounding green areas

This study further revealed a negative correlation between animal
diversity and surrounding green areas at 100 m and 1000 m from the
investigated walls, which stipulates the importance of building VGSs for
increased biodiversity benefits, in particular for an increased number of
species. Animal abundance positively correlated to the availability of
green area at distances between 50 m to 1000 m from the VGSs and
control walls. This suggests that even green areas located at larger dis-
tances from VGSs might serve as stepping stones for animals that
migrate over longer distances and use building VGSs as a temporary or
permanent habitat. Therefore, our research confirms the need for more
stepping stone habitats within urban environments to increase biodi-
versity. When implementing VGSs on buildings it is, therefore, critical to
plan for urban connectivity and functionally link VGSs to neighbouring
VGSs or other type of vegetation, for instance to nearby trees, urban
parks or green roofs [56].

4.6. Limitations & future work

Limitations of this research include that biodiversity assessments
could not be done exhaustively for the entire wall surface but had to be
focused on their lower part for accessibility reasons despite that binoc-
ulars and cameras were used. It cannot be excluded that other animals
inhabit VGSs at higher distance from the ground. Yet, species richness
especially of crawling and climbing animals reduces with height due to
higher energy efforts to forage in higher areas [57,58]. Future studies
could also include testing of other methods for biodiversity identifica-
tion such as non-destructive insect traps as they might capture more
animals and individuals than the observational method achieved in this
study [57,59]. Furthermore, future research is recommended that also
quantifies other potential ecosystem services generated on VGSs such as
food provisioning, carbon storage and sequestration as well as cultural
services like aesthetics and thermal comfort [60]. Other factors such as
solar radiation and human activity around the VGSs may also have an
impact on the VGS ecosystem service performance, however, these were
not quantified in this study. Lastly, we could not quantify management
regimes but we foresee the type of VGS management (e.g., fogging with
insecticides, vegetation trimming intensity) have a huge impact on
biodiversity.
Many indicators and models for ecosystem services quantification
exist for non-urban ecosystems, however a unifying consensus on in-
dicators and benchmarks for ecological performance of buildings
(including VGSs) is still lacking [61–63]. Computed models that are
currently being used to measure urban ecosystem services are generally
only based on a few empirical data that were collected in non-urban or
controlled environments often resulting in inaccurate modeling of
ecosystem services [64,65]. Therefore, more empirical data collection
efforts are required by replicating our study design in different locations
and climates. Unifying and comparing such data could deliver a reliable
source for successful urban VGS design on buildings that provide a
multitude of benefits to humans, biodiversity and the environment.

5. Conclusions

Our results also show that despite occupying a relatively small
footprint on the ground, VGSs can host a variety of species (291 species
were observed across walls). VGSs can also have a cooling effect for the
building wall during the day, while having an insulating effect during
the night. This study thus provided evidence that VGSs in Singapore
have a positive impact on habitat provisioning for animals as well as on
air temperature regulation on buildings. It also presented the nuances
such that resulting benefits of an implemented VGS depend on factors
