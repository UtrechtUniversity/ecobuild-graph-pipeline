Case Study

Response of Construction Clients to
Low-Carbon Building Regulations

Beliz Ozorhon1

Abstract: Building regulations and governmental policies create pressure on the construction industry to minimize the environmental
impacts that are caused by buildings. Achieving energy efficiency, and thereby low-carbon construction, is only possible by cooperation
among the participants in the construction supply chain. Clients in particular have an important role in promoting environmental sus-
tainability. In this paper, the carbon-reduction process is analyzed from a construction client’s perspective. A framework is developed to
analyze the interrelated components of this process, including the drivers, initiatives, challenges, facilitators, solutions, and achievements.
A qualitative case study methodology was employed to gain a better understanding of the project-based practices and corporate policies
in achieving energy efficiency. The study presents the results of three award-winning construction projects from the U.K. in which
innovations for improving environmental sustainability were based on the Code for Sustainable Homes. The study outlines the initiatives
and strategies of clients for carbon reduction, obstacles faced by project teams, mechanisms that helped overcome the difficulties, and
low-carbon solutions applied to design, construction, and operation in the selected cases. Effective leadership, corporate responsibility,
a focus on end users, repeatable nature of projects, and integrated teams are found to be the main success factors. The findings of this
study are expected to guide construction professionals in implementing innovative low-carbon practices in collaborative project envi-
ronments. The proposed framework can be used to better understand the carbon-reduction process in other projects and other countries

This paper presents the results of three case studies examining
the role of building regulations and government policies in engag-
ing construction clients in carbon-reduction initiatives and the
role of clients in turn in promoting environmental sustainability. In
this respect, three award-winning construction projects, completed
in the northwest United Kingdom (U.K.), were selected as case

second one concerns an eco-friendly supermarket project, and the

government policies involved and analyzes their impact, summa-
rizes the key actions taken by the clients in promoting environmen-

discusses the challenges and supporting mechanisms throughout
the process, and finally presents the achievements of the projects.
The findings of this study are expected to help participants in the
construction industry develop a better understanding of the factors
contributing to success in the carbon-reduction process.

With strong evidence of the impact of climate change on the planet
emerging and with the built environment and construction activ-
ities significantly contributing to waste, energy use, and greenhouse
gas emissions [Dept. for Communities and Local Government
(DCLG) 2007], environmental sustainability has become a major
concern for the construction industry. As a response to climate
change and its effects on the environment and energy consumption,
academics, practitioners, and governmental bodies have been in-
volved in many discussions and applications to create a low-carbon
economy. A growing emphasis on corporate sustainability has
caused governments, clients, and stakeholder groups to exert in-
creasing pressure on the construction industry to become more
accountable for its environmental impacts (Thorpe et al. 2008).
Energy efficiency requires the development of innovative
solutions, ranging from transforming design to improving quality
and applying new processes, such as modern methods of construc-
tion (DCLG 2008). Considering the increasing need for a sustain-
able built environment, building regulations have been reported as

Research Background

when they have committed to corporate social responsibility.
The delivery process for sustainable buildings is more complex
than traditional construction (Lapinski et al. 2006; Korkmaz
et al. 2010). Robichaud and Anantatmula (2011) mention the need
for several modifications in traditional project management practi-
ces to deliver innovative and green construction. Such projects
require integration in the delivery process by an early involvement
of the contractor and an owner commitment toward sustain-
ability (Beheiry et al. 2006; Swarup et al. 2011; Robichaud and
Anantatmula 2011; Mollaoglu-Korkmaz et al. 2013). Similarly,
Hakkinen and Belloni (2011) argue that traditional contractual
arrangements may cause mutual distrust and lack of communica-
tion and thereby inhibit the identification and implementation of
new products and processes. They further assert that these barriers
to the adoption of innovation are particularly severe in the case of
sustainable buildings that have high costs and high risks. Manage-
ment of innovation is complicated by the discontinuous nature of
project-based production in which, often, there are broken learning
and feedback loops (Barlow 2000). As Blayse and Manley (2004)
stated, “the one-off nature of most building projects limits the de-
gree to which a given innovation will be applicable to other situa-
tions, reducing the benefits of innovation and therefore incentives
to innovate.” A study by Gann and Salter (2000) points out the need
for a better conceptual understanding of innovation and new man-
agement practices to link project and business processes. Business
processes are ongoing and repetitive, whereas project processes
have a tendency to be temporary and unique (Gann 1998; Brusoni
et al. 1998). However, in the case of long-term relationships with
clients, contractors secure repeat business and have the opportunity
to apply the same or similar innovations in other projects as well.
This motivates contractors to invest in new technologies and help
them transfer their knowledge and experience in future projects.
This research examines three projects in which innovative so-
lutions are applied for carbon reduction by exploring the role of
clients in cultivating these solutions.

Construction affects carbon emission throughout the life cycle of
a building, including the design, manufacture, construction, oper-
ation, refurbishment, and disposal stages. Buildings contribute to
approximately 40% of carbon emission in the world (Shorrock et al.
2005). A similar figure is valid for the U.K. The final report of the
Low Carbon Construction Innovation and Growth Team reported
that domestic and nondomestic buildings are responsible for 27 and
18% of the U.K.’s total emissions, respectively (Dept. for Business,
Innovation and Skills 2010). The U.K. is among the countries
highly committed to reducing carbon and other greenhouse gas
emissions with a number of regulations and initiatives. Under the
Climate Change Act of 2008, the U.K. is committed to reducing
greenhouse gas emissions by 80% by 2050 (DCLG 2007). All
new housing in the U.K. has been subject to carbon-reduction
regulations since the implementation of Part L of the Building
Regulations, which was first issued in 2002 and revised in 2006
and 2010. Based on the Code for Sustainable Homes (CSH), the
U.K. government has announced targets for all new housing to
be zero carbon by 2016 and new commercial buildings by
2019. The Building a Greener Future U.K. government policy
in particular states that all new homes need to achieve a 25%
improvement in energy/carbon performance by 2010, a 44% im-
provement by 2013, and a zero-carbon target by 2016, with zero
carbon being defined as one where the net carbon dioxide emis-
sions resulting from all energy used in a building are zero or better
(DCLG 2007).
As Hakkinen and Belloni (2011) state, “there are different
strategies to pursue (either with help of reducing consumption,
improving efficiency or developing substitutive and less harmful
solutions), but in all cases innovations are needed.” As a result,
the carbon-reduction process in buildings involves successful inno-
vation implementation. A number of studies investigated the con-
ditions under which sustainable and zero-carbon buildings can be
achieved through innovations. For example, Dewick and Miozzo
(2004) analyzed the diffusion of sustainable technologies in the
Scottish housing market. Osmani and O’Reilly (2009) explored
the views of housebuilders in England on the drivers for and bar-
riers to zero-carbon home building. In a similar study, Hakkinen
and Belloni (2011) examined the barriers and drivers for sustain-
able buildings in Finland and concluded that the demand and
willingness of clients eventually determines the development of
sustainable buildings. The more demanding and experienced the
client, the more likely it is to stimulate innovation in projects it
commissions (Barlow 2000). Clients that conduct internal research
and development (R&D) or design projects themselves, have a
history of innovation, or maintain long-term relationships with
the same designers/contractors are found to be most likely to set
the preconditions for innovative behavior on a project (Nam and
Tatum 1997).
In the Rethinking Construction report, Egan (1998) emphasized
the importance of committed leadership, focus on the customer,
integrated processes and teams, a quality-driven agenda, and com-
mitment to people as the key drivers of excellence and innovation
in construction. The literature on sustainable construction discusses
many of these factors among the enablers of innovative solutions.
For example, Tatum (1987), Barlow (2000), and Aronson et al.
(2006) referred to leadership as one of the main enablers of
innovation. Ozorhon et al. (2010) emphasized the importance of
understanding customer requirements for successful adoption
of construction innovations. Another issue is related to corporate
social responsibility. As Hakkinen and Belloni (2011) state, retail-

Research Methodology

In this research, a qualitative case study methodology was adopted
to gain a better understanding of project-based practices and
corporate policies on achieving energy-efficient solutions. Case
studies try to answer the how and why questions in a research study,
allowing a more in-depth analysis (Yin 2003). Four quality mea-
sures are required to successfully conduct case studies, as explained
in Yin (2003): (1) construct validity (the quality of conceptualiza-
tion or operationalization of the relevant concept), (2) internal
validity (the causal relationships between variables and results),
(3) external validity (the extent to which the finding can be gener-
alized), and (4) reliability (repeatability with the same results).
To improve the construct validity, both archival data and inter-
view data are used in the analysis. A research framework was es-
tablished based on the literature to satisfy the internal validity. To
address the external validity, multiple case studies were performed,
and these were selected among the award-winning projects. A case
study database was constructed (including interview transcripts
and all available documents) to allow replication of the study and
ensure sufficient reliability. Steps of the case study approach are
explained subsequently in detail.

ducted by Ozorhon et al. (2010), who investigated the construction
innovation process at the project level. In this respect, the Centre for
Construction Innovation (CCI) in northwestern U.K., the regional

Ozorhon et al. (2010) developed a framework to investigate the
innovation process within a construction project setting based on
several components including the drivers, inputs, barriers, enablers,
innovative activities, and outputs. Based on this previous work, a
similar framework (Fig. 1) was employed to investigate the carbon-
reduction process in selected projects. The main components of
the carbon-reduction process are (1) environmental sustainability
driver, (2) client-led carbon-reduction initiatives, (3) challenges,
(4) facilitators, (5) low-carbon solutions, and (6) project achieve-
ments. In this process, the solutions are shaped based on the drivers,
initiatives, challenges, and facilitators, and the solutions lead to the
achievements.
Semistructured interviews were conducted with the respondents
to identify the roles of clients in environmental sustainability issues
in selected cases. Based on the preceding framework, the following
questions were discussed during the interviews:
•
What were the main reasons/drivers to improve environmental
sustainability?
•
What was the role of the client in terms of initiating and leading
the process?
•
What were the main challenges that the project team experi-
enced during the process?
•
What helped overcome those challenges?
•
What were the main low-carbon solutions introduced within the
project?
•
What were the main benefits that the project team achieved as a
result of those solutions?
The experiences in selected projects were explored through a
series of half-day interviews with a number of participants from
the client, contractor, and design organizations. In the first case
of the urban regeneration project, the interviewees were a perfor-
mance and quality manager from the client, a project manager from
the contractor, and a business development manager from the con-
tractor. In the second case of the eco-friendly supermarket, the in-
terview participants were the property services director from the
client, a civil engineer from the contractor, and the manager of

the technology center that acted as a consultant. In the third case
of the eco-residences, the interviewees were the director of the
estates from the client, the product development manager from
the residential developer, a civil engineer from the contractor,
and the head of the design team from the architect. Participation
of relevant parties in these project-based interviews was critical
in terms of obtaining contradictory opinions or commonalities
based on each stakeholder’s viewpoint. In this way, it was possible
to examine and discuss the interaction of the actors involved in the
projects. The CCI provided the application forms for the awards,
which describe the details of each project. Some corporate docu-
ments, including environmental policies, waste management plans,
and environmental performance records, were shared by interview-
ees. The tape-recorded interview data were transcribed and com-
bined with other documents to capture the role of clients in the
carbon-reduction process in each case.
The case studies provide some key lessons on how to success-
fully implement energy-efficient solutions as a response to environ-
mental sustainability requirements in construction, and therefore
the studies are expected to shed light on future projects and their
corresponding participants. In addition, the themes that emerge
based on the analysis of the cases suggest some mechanisms and
strategies that might benefit the project stakeholders when adopting
similar solutions. The cases are analyzed based on the six compo-
nents of the framework presented in Fig. 1.

Case I

The regeneration project in Cheshire, U.K., was a unique 10-year
partnering regeneration scheme that has delivered over 500 units of
new housing as part of the vision for a sustainable future for the
area. The client of the project is a housing association that owns
and manages homes across the U.K.. The contractor, the architect,
consultants, and the structural engineers were brought into the part-
nership in late 2001 to undertake the housing regeneration phases.
The regeneration partnership has used more than £44 million of
funding to completely revitalize this estate, which was first built
between 1968 and 1972. The master plan proposed a comprehen-
sive program of over 50 individual projects from the redesign of
infrastructure and transport to community facilities and modern
mixed-tenure housing.

Sustainability Driver

The building regulations on social housing had a great impact on
the processes and performance of this regeneration project. The
U.K. government’s initiative to create sustainable homes is speci-
fied in the CSH. This code requires that contractors use innovative
products in their construction processes and deliver the specified
sustainability performance levels. Funding conditions set by the
Housing Corporation promoted the increased use of modern meth-
ods of construction (MMC) in social housing, particularly off-site
manufacturing (OSM), as a key potential method for promoting
sustainability within the construction industry. This is also evi-
denced in Osmani and O’Reilly’s (2009) study that found legisla-
tion as the strongest driver of zero-carbon homes.

Client-Led Carbon-Reduction Initiatives

The partnership embraced the principles outlined in the Rethinking
Construction report by Egan (1998). The report showed that radical
changes in construction processes could deliver real cost savings
(up to 30%), eliminate waste in the delivery process, encourage
innovation, and allow participants to learn from experience

Low-Carbon Solutions

The timber frame is a tried and tested structural system. It is the
most environmentally friendly form of construction available that
conforms to MMC and OSM principles. The Building Research
Establishment (BRE) reported that modern timber frame construc-
tion produces near zero carbon emissions (Reynolds and Enjily
2005). Timber frames are also renowned for their excellence in
energy-efficiency terms. As the structures are assembled from com-
ponents made to manufacturing tolerances, the better fit achieved
improves air tightness and, hence, positively affects energy effi-
ciency. Closed timber frames were used as the structural elements
of the superstructure in the project among the many forms to
choose from, including advanced and closed panel, volumetric, and
hybrid systems. The benefits of MMC were evidenced in many
ways; not only was the site accident free, it was dryer and cleaner
for the labor force to work in. There were also benefits for local
residents too, with reduced levels of vehicle movement and noise.
The team focused on identifying waste-causing activities; it stand-
ardized the finishing processes and reduced wastage of materials on
site as well as wasted operations.

Challenges

Based on more than 2 years of R&D work, cost, knowledge, and
familiarity were identified as the main barriers to the adoption
of MMC. MMC and lean construction involve totally different
operations that initially threatened the entire industry. Not only did
contractors face challenges in shifting to new processes but subcon-
tractors had to follow at the same pace and adjust operations based
on site conditions, which were not always favorable. Construction
in this way was far ahead of normal schedules due to the unexpect-
edly quick installations of the timber frames, and the construction
team had difficulties in allocating a sufficient number of staff to
continue with the finishings. It was initially difficult to control such
a tight process, and the intensity of the workforce was a real issue.
Another problem was related to the unavailability of advanced
products and the reluctance of most suppliers to work on them
due to the increased costs. Cost has been reported as one of the
main barriers to low-carbon homes in other studies as well
(e.g., Dewick and Miozzo 2004; Hakkinen and Belloni 2011).

Project Achievements

It was estimated that the use of MMC achieved waste reductions
in excess of 50%. Noise levels were also 50% less than in a tradi-
tional build. Using timber frames, the team achieved higher levels
of environmental sustainability; it enhanced thermal performance
(with lower U values) and decreased the impact on the environment
through lower carbon emissions—a 6% reduction in waste to
landfill and 74% increase in waste recycled against 10% targets.
The project was awarded the Building Research Establishment
Environmental Assessment Method (BREEAM) very good rating.
BREEAM is an environmental assessment method and rating
system for buildings and was launched in 1990 in the U.K. by
the BRE.

Facilitators

The client determined to work with the contractor to eliminate the
initial cost barriers of MMC in order to reap long-term savings and
benefits. The key decision in this regard was to use a procurement
approach that enabled early contractor involvement. This ensured
that ideas could be tested for their buildability and building meth-
ods developed that were appropriate for the design. The project
team identified that the maximum benefits of MMC would only be
achieved through the application of lean principles, on which they
received consultancy. The trust and good communication created
through partnering enabled the joint utilization of resources. The
client and the contractor also managed to establish a partnership
with a timber frame supplier that had invested in R&D on environ-
mental sustainability and that could produce the required products.
The Lean Construction report by Constructing Excellence (2004)
in the U.K. also emphasized the role of integrated design-build
arrangements, including partnering and supply chain management
for increasing the effectiveness of the industry.
One of the most important ingredients of any successful
regeneration partnership is the involvement of the local community.
Residents of the area have been heavily involved in the regenera-
tion process. Project team members have had the opportunity to
establish positive working relationships with the local community
and better understand the needs and wants of the residents affected
by the redevelopment. The client, architect, and contractor have
also worked with local primary schools to involve children in the
construction processes as part of their curriculum.

Case II

The environmental format store was part of a major regeneration
scheme within the Cheetham Hill area in northwestern U.K.
It was special because it was the first repeatable format to be built
for one of the major food retailers in the U.K., with the aim of
becoming standard design for future stores. As the interviewees
mentioned, the scheme is expected to reduce operational energy by
70%, far in excess of the original 50% target. A mixture of new
construction techniques was employed to deliver an environmen-
tally friendly, energy-efficient, sustainable, innovative, integrated,
and value-added project while achieving client satisfaction. The
low-carbon solutions were selected through a number of design
workshops and tests during the procurement stage of the project.

Sustainability Driver

This project is a very good example of a client-driven approach to
energy efficiency. What makes the project even more special is that
the client is a private investor. The client initiated the process
by recognizing the importance of delivering high environmental
performance. The first action of the client was to set an agenda
to reduce carbon emissions throughout its operations. The major
idea was to deliver an environmentally friendly format store that

The client put corporate responsibility and the impact of its
buildings and operations on the environment at the top of
its agenda. The client’s stated aim is to reduce emissions from its
buildings by at least 50% by 2020. The client worked with a team
of specialist consultants and started an organized plan. It also
wanted to be a leader in its market by first reducing CO2 emissions
in all of its operations and then sharing the acquired knowledge on
innovation with other organizations (including competitors) and
government to help stimulate a low-carbon economy. This would
enhance the corporate image of the client and help it lead the
market in green business. This environmental store was not the first
attempt of the client; it had been building a series of environmental
stores in every country it operates in since 2006. It has been testing
new technologies, designs, and construction materials to save
energy and reduce its carbon footprint. This store was important
for the client since it would serve as the client’s new blueprint
for future stores, the new blueprint being designed to work across
all formats.

Apart from the client’s commitment to carbon reduction, another
key success factor was the collaborative partnership. The client had
worked previously on developing and implementing green technol-
ogies with its partners and supply chains. The client established a
strategic alliance with its contractors in 1985. It has a total of nine
contractors (including the contractor of the project under investiga-
tion) working with them, all of which carry out R&D on their
behalf. This decentralized research approach has brought the value
chain mutual benefits. Teams having specific expertise have con-
tinued to invest in developing new solutions for the client. The con-
tinuous (repeatable) nature of the projects was the most significant
factor that encouraged the alliance partners to innovate.
As the interview participants noted, the concept development for
an environmentally friendly format store started in 2001 and in-
volved collaborative work among all project team members, includ-
ing a representative from the client, engineer, architect, services
consultant, and contractor. Their aim was to achieve a repeatable
store design that could be built anywhere in the U.K. in a cost-
effective manner. The contracting company started working with
the client during the conceptual phase, 1 year prior to construction.
Early contractor involvement is observed to be an important suc-
cess factor in this respect.

Client-Led Carbon-Reduction Initiatives

Since the client wanted to play a leadership role in tackling climate
change, it aimed at mobilizing collective action among customers,
suppliers, and employees to help protect the environment and gen-
erate a mass movement in green consumption. The climate change
program the client introduced had three main parts: (1) leading by
example (reducing its own direct carbon footprint), (2) working
with its supply chains and partners to reduce emissions more
broadly, and (3) leading a revolution in green business.
Initiated with ambitious targets, the client’s attempts to reduce
carbon were followed by extensive R&D work with an investment
of £100 million. This fund was established in 2007 to develop
sustainable technologies with the aim of carbon reduction in
its stores, distribution centers, and supply chains worldwide. In
2008, the client invested an additional amount of approximately
£86 million in energy-saving and low-carbon technologies, includ-
ing 47 combined heat and power (CHP) and combined cooling,
heat, and power (CCHP) units and 27 wind turbines. The expected
carbon emission saving of these technologies is approximately
6,000 tons per annum.

Low-Carbon Solutions

The construction of the eco-store was based on a number of prin-
ciples including using wood instead of metal; introducing more
natural daylight into the store to use less electricity; achieving a
more energy-efficient heating and air-conditioning system; using
less energy and water; generating its own energy; rethinking refrig-
eration, fixtures, and signage; and minimizing waste, all of which
contributed significantly in reducing the store’s carbon footprint.
The team worked on a number of energy-efficient and sustainable
solutions, most of which were developed specifically for this
project. The use of a hybrid timber-steel frame developed by a
manufacturer with expertise in steel structures was a unique and
innovative approach. The scheme is the first supermarket to run
on vegetable oil via a CHP unit that provides the energy and heat
requirement of the store. The scheme includes many other environ-
mentally friendly innovations, such as a certified hybrid timber
frame, a sustainable cladding system, a recycled and prefabricated
walling system, a premanufactured polycarbonate roof light system
a CHP unit, CO2 refrigeration, wind catchers, and CO2 sensors, all
of which help to reduce energy usage. These are described in more
detail in what follows:
•
Hybrid timber frame: one of the most important elements of the
environmentally friendly design features is the innovative hy-
brid timber-steel structure provided by the supplier. It combined
the aesthetic and environmental benefits of timber with the
economic benefits of steel that reduce carbon footprint without
compromising quality.
•
Sustainable cladding system: the system was sourced from
Lithuania and required a lot of detailing and planning to execute
a very smooth installation without any problems. It was man-
ufactured off site and offers an embedded/carbon energy saving
of 8%, and the cladding is 100% recyclable.
•
TriArch walling solution: this system is a prefabricated walling
system, which is made from recycled gypsum and timber board-
ing. This product is 100% recyclable; there is no requirement
for plastering because the system is prefinished and ready for
either wall paper or paint finish, which ultimately results in
no wastage.

Challenges

Throughout the project, each project member had his/her own
concerns, contributions, and expectations. The client acted as
the leader of the project at each stage. The architect had a specific
interest in the external appearance and aesthetics of the building.
The cost consultant worked on ensuring a cost-effective solution.
The structural engineer and supplier worked together to develop
a tailored timber frame. Finally, the contractor, being a strategic
partner with the client, was concerned with achieving customer
satisfaction.
Products that would comply with the design objectives were
scarce. During the product development stage, the project team
searched the market for available products and spent considerable
effort on converting these into a form ready for use in the new store.
Although this led to a cost increase, it pushed other suppliers to
work together in accommodating the changes. One of the chal-
lenges in this process was that the team struggled to convince all
suppliers to change their processes; however, helping a supplier to
access a big market empowered them to allocate resources to tailor
its products for future use.

Client-Led Carbon-Reduction Initiatives

The university hosts two environmental research centers aiming
to develop environmental strategies and implement those strategies
in an effective manner. The university has also commissioned a
Sustainable Energy Infrastructure Study as part of its corporate
objectives, which is set to inform the ongoing development of envi-
ronmental policy. It tries to adopt the following main principles in
its operations: reduction of greenhouse gas emissions; optimization
of energy and water efficiency; reduction of waste, travel, and pol-
lution; application of environmental best practice in construction
projects; selection of supply chain members accordingly; efficiency
in resource use, increase in recycling; implementation of an envi-
ronmental management system (for ISO14001 certification); and
collaboration with other universities to spread environmental
awareness and support relevant research.
The university established a partnership with a residential devel-
oper experienced in the design, building, funding, management,
and operation of residential student housing. The university played
a key role in establishing another partnership aimed at helping
departments across campus to promote and deliver environmental
initiatives. It has helped increase recycling rates, reduce toxic
waste, reduce energy consumption, and green the university’s sup-
ply chain. In addition, it has generated jobs for students and raised
the profile of environmental issues among students and staff. It has
also aimed at involving end-users (students) in the process to under-
stand their requirements better early in the design phase.

Project Achievements

The client’s commitment to environmental sustainability through a
combination of new technology and good business resulted in dra-
matic reductions in its carbon footprint. The eco-store’s carbon
footprint is 70% less than an equivalent store built in 2006. This
was achieved through the use of roof lights allowing more natural
daylight into the store, saving on electricity (31%); CO2 refrigerant
(20%); and a combined heat and power plant running on recycled
vegetable oil (19%). The project was awarded the BREEAM very
good rating with the energy consumption levels achieved.

Challenges

For the construction phase, panelized timber frames were used for
sustainability purposes. However, timber frame construction was
new to some members of the construction team. The inexperience
and conservative behavior of the contractor were an obstacle in the
innovation process. To overcome those issues, seminars and project
meetings were held at an early stage to familiarize the contractor
and supply chain partners with the process approach and construc-
tion technology involved.

Case III

The eco-friendly accommodation on a university campus was the
first replicable scalable model of its kind. It was designed to create
a social space for students that would encourage good habits in
terms of sustainable living. The university’s eco-residences were
developed to address issues and problems identified from three
previous phases of new-build residences at the university. The uni-
versity also seeks to develop future estates on the basis of sustain-
able principles.

Facilitators

With the end-user requirements being an important component of
the scheme, engagement with students began at an early stage in the
design process. This aspect was considerably strengthened by the
measures in place to monitor and provide information on energy
use as a means of directly involving students with the ongoing
operation of the building. A competition was launched among the
students to promote and incentivize reduced water, gas, and elec-
tricity usage and reward the townhouses with the lowest resultant
carbon footprint.
The partnership with the residential developer collaborated
with the architect that prepared a bespoke design adopting the
sustainable-by-design concept by Friedman (2007), a Canadian
academic and authority on the subject of affordable housing. The
key features of the sustainable-by-design concept focused on a
simple design delivering high energy efficiency and heat recovery.
Since the architect was experienced in social housing and, thus, in
low-carbon practices, his design principles were found appropriate
for this project.

Sustainability Driver

The project was notable for the proactive role of the client, whose
desire and commitment to sustainable practices was the main driver
of carbon reduction. This drive was itself reinforced through energy
performance regulation, such as the CSH and specific guidance
by the Higher Education Funding Council for England (HEFCE).
The CSH requires contractors to use innovative products in their
construction processes to deliver specified sustainable performance
levels. Similarly, HEFCE guidance on universities’ environmental
performance provides an important benchmark. HEFCE has con-

Project Achievements

Low-Carbon Solutions

The project team achieved both environmentally high-performing
residences and affordable construction. The cost was decreased
by 10% per student room in comparison to the previous phase.
The rent charged to students was reduced by 15% against the rental
charges in the previous phase. The annual CO2 emission per
student was reduced by 20% against design criteria set by the
Building Regulations. The CO2 emission is predicted to be
963 kg CO2=annum=student compared to the design target of
1,147 kg CO2=annum=student. Finally, gas consumption was re-
duced by 5–10% per student room compared to the previous-phase
residences. The project received a BREEAM rating of excellent for
its high-quality example of good environmental design.

of the proposed framework can be found in Table 1.

The eco-residences were designed and constructed to achieve lower
CO2 emission rates than required by Building Regulations. The
following list includes the details of low-carbon solutions in the
eco-residence scheme:
•
Plan: the designs were based on the use of modular dimensions,
which allow the use of building components to their manufac-
tured sizes, such as plasterboard or ply sheets. This speeds up
the construction process, because there is minimum require-
ment for cutting and fitting of components, and significantly
reduces waste.
•
Energy and resource efficiency: the design of the building, com-
prising terraced units, has been very efficient in terms of site
utilization (good floor-to-wall ratio), minimization of external
surfaces (high levels of energy efficiency), and minimization
of material use. Efficient use of electricity was designed in
through maximum use of natural light, minimizing lighting re-
quirements. Low-energy fluorescent light fittings were used
throughout the buildings with passive infrared detectors in com-
munal areas. Water use was minimized by low-flow-rate fittings
and dual flush fittings on toilets.
•
Sustainable and renewable materials: the reduction of waste
is central to the environmental sustainability concept, and the
choice of construction materials is highly linked to carbon
footprint reduction. The BRE’s Green Guide to Specification
(Anderson et al. 2009), which is part of BREEAM, was used
to inform the choice of materials, and benign natural materials
were selected wherever possible to ensure a healthy internal en-
vironment. By using timber sourced from sustainable, managed
forests for the building’s superstructure, the carbon footprint
was reduced by 30%. Timber frames were also manufactured
off site to minimize waste. Recycled construction materials were
used as much as possible in underlying hard-paved areas and
floor-pads. Internal fittings such as showers, basins, and lava-
tories were assembled off site as complete pods, improving con-
struction speed and reducing construction impact further.
•
Heat recovery: a passive approach was used to create a very
highly insulated, airtight building envelope that requires very
little energy for space heating. The major energy load is hot
water generation, which is supplemented by solar thermal
panels. A heat recovery system, operating on the extract venti-
lation serving the showers/bathrooms and providing warm air to
the internal circulation areas, was installed. Each room was
equipped with a small radiator, controlled by a thermostatic
radiator valve and linked to a high-efficiency, gas-condensing
boiler. Ventilation with heat recovery is primarily whole-house
mechanical ventilation with heat recovery (reclaiming 70%).
Trickle vents and windows that can be opened to provide addi-
tional user controllable ventilation were installed. The kitchen
cooker extractors switch on and off automatically.
•
Utility monitoring: the university recognizes the significant
environmental impacts associated with its operations. It also re-
cognizes its responsibility to address these through its environ-
mental policy and environmental management system (EMS).
The environmental impact of new or refurbished buildings is
very carefully evaluated from conception through design and
construction to operation. Each unit was fitted with a building
management system (BMS) that constantly monitors and com-
municates water, gas, and electricity use to enable students to
monitor the carbon footprint for their rooms. It is notable that
the BMS has raised the awareness of students in terms of
sustainability and has become an integral component of their
daily lives.

Discussion

Clients are themselves the end users, or they represent the end users
in construction, so demand is shaped by clients; therefore, they play
a significant role in driving sustainability. However, the ways in
which public and private sector clients perceive environmentally
conscious construction is not entirely the same. The main motiva-
tion for the public sector is the environmental sustainability regu-
lations, whereas it is a better corporate image that motivates the
private sector. The success factors of the carbon-reduction process
in three selected projects were quite similar and supportive of pre-
vious research on sustainable buildings and innovation. Five main
themes emerge based on the findings of this study. These are the
client’s leadership style, client corporate responsibility, focus on
end users, repeatability of projects, and integrated teams.
1. Leadership: leadership has been referred to as one of the main
enablers of innovation in the construction literature (Tatum
1987; Ozorhon et al. 2010). All three of the cases emphasize
the importance of key leadership of clients and their devotion
to sustainability. In the first case of urban regeneration, the
client introduced the ideas to improve environmental sustain-
ability performance based on the CSH. Because the client was
experienced in terms of MMC, it guided the project team
through the use of collaborative partnering. The retail client,
on the other hand, aimed at producing an eco-supermarket
and wanted to play a leadership role in responding to climate
change. In the third case, the university aimed at designing and
constructing the first eco-friendly student accommodations in
the U.K. and becoming an exemplar in this respect. Among the
most important objectives of all clients in investing in carbon
reduction was to serve as a user of best practices. They were
also willing to share their experience and knowledge with
other organizations to raise awareness about environmental
sustainability. They managed to construct cost-effective build-
ings and showed how a green agenda could be achieved suc-
cessfully. Such clients made significant achievements with
their teams and encourage other clients as well.
2. Corporate responsibility: in line with the aforementioned lea-
dership issue, all clients, especially the university and the retail
client, put environmental and social corporate responsibility as
their top priorities in their operations. First of all, they took
legislation very seriously, including HEFCE guidelines in the
third case and CSH in all three cases. Secondly they have their
own environmental policies and waste management plans and
were strongly committed to carbon reduction. All projects
were awarded by BREEAM for their high environmental per-
formance levels.

3. Focus on end user: in all examples, the clients were driven to
provide better services for their end users. In the regeneration
project, the overall objective was to improve the quality of life
in the region. By engaging the residents early in the design
phase, the project team satisfied end-user needs and expecta-
tions. The client in the second case is renowned for being
a customer-oriented organization. This point is particularly
significant because innovative solutions for carbon reduction
cannot be implemented only to meet regulations. Building reg-
ulations provide guidance and act as a driver of such solutions
to a certain extent; however, this is not sufficient, especially in
terms of private clients. If there is strong focus on end users
and creating value for them, this drive will be critical for the
success of the process. In the third case, the university’s initial
objective was to create good habits among students. It engaged
students in the project starting from the design phase; it highly
valued student input and tried to create living spaces to satisfy
the students. It also organized a carbon competition to enhance
student contributions. This was also evidenced in Barrett’s
(2007) work, which stated that project teams aiming at enga-
ging end users from the early phases of projects designed for
public use are more likely to achieve end-user satisfaction.
4. Repeatability: another key point in all cases was that all the
projects were designed as replicable projects. This brings
an additional advantage in construction projects, when the
project parties make sure that same design could be applied
in subsequent projects. In the case of the urban regeneration,
further phases will follow; therefore, the same principles will
be applied in those phases. The eco-store was planned to be
a blueprint for all future stores. Therefore, knowledge and
experience gained throughout the project, starting from design
through construction to operation, were critical. The repeata-
ble nature of the project was also expected to increase the like-
lihood of winning planning permission from environmentally
conscious local authorities to build new sustainable stores in
the future. In the third case, a similar situation was observed.
The university aimed at creating the first replicable, scalable
model of its kind. It will continue to develop future estates on
the basis of sustainable principles and benefit from this pro-
ject. Repeat business and integration of the experience gained
on projects into business processes help companies become
learning organizations, as observed in one of the case studies
conducted in Miozzo and Dewick’s (2002) research.
5. Integrated teams: integration along the supply chain has been
one of the essential ingredients of the successful outcome in
all three cases. In the first case, a regeneration partnership
has been working together for 10 years in harmony, and this
helped the partners build trust among each other and make
joint investments in lean consultancy. Such a collaborative
approach has been the most critical facilitator of the process.
In the case of the second project, integration was observed
even more widely. The strategic partnership between the client
and the contractor created an environment conducive to testing
innovative products for their buildability. Early contractor
involvement was essential to ensure the success of proposed
solutions. Increased integration and trust-based cooperation
through early involvement of contractors are believed to help
team members achieve efficient and value-adding solutions
(Barlow et al. 1997; Briscoe et al. 2004; Swarup et al. 2011;
Robichaud and Anantatmula 2011; Mollaoglu-Korkmaz et al.
2013). The client’s approach to partnering with multiple con-
tractors can also be regarded as an innovative approach. This
also helped the partnering contractors become more competi-
tive and at the same time share knowledge and innovations.

Conclusions

References

This paper investigated the carbon-reduction process from a
construction client’s perspective. In this context, a framework
was developed to analyze six different components of this process,
including the drivers, initiatives, challenges, facilitators, solutions,
and achievements. A case study methodology was employed to
understand the project-based innovative solutions and corporate
strategies in three award-winning construction projects. The analy-
sis of the cases based on the research framework indicated that
(1) building regulations and client requirements were the major
reasons to enhance the environmental performance levels in the
projects, (2) clients invested in R&D jointly with their contractor
partners to provide low carbon solutions, (3) resistance of team
members and unavailability of products were major barriers to in-
novation, (4) most challenges were overcome through partnering
along the supply chain, (5) innovative designs and construction
methods were used to improve sustainability, and (6) considerable
energy efficiency was achieved.
A better understanding of the factors determining the success of
a carbon-reduction process and the role of clients in leading this
process can be considered the main contribution of this study. Five
main themes emerged based on the findings of those three cases.
Firstly, all clients demonstrated effective leadership; they guided
the project teams in adopting sustainability principles and energy-
efficient solutions. In this respect, they had characteristics similar
to those of leading-edge clients with high levels of technical com-
petence along with challenging needs and extensive experience
(Morrison et al. 2004). Secondly, the clients put social and envi-
ronmental corporate responsibility at the top of their priority lists;
they wanted to contribute to community life by enhancing the qual-
ity of life and creating jobs and to decrease their environmental
impact through their own environmental policies. Thirdly, they
focused on the needs of their end users and concentrated on the
value added by their projects. Fourthly, a major motivation of such
a challenging process that involved innovative solutions was the
repeatable nature of all the projects. This was critical in terms
of ensuring the long-term benefits of the investments made and
efforts expended. Finally, successful adoption of low-carbon solu-
tions was achieved through integration along the supply chains.
In all cases, partnering among the project participants helped them
build a strong team culture.
The benefits of sustainable practices were observed at the
project level in terms of higher environmental performance, lower
costs, and higher quality and at a broader level in terms of enhanc-
ing the clients’ reputation. Due to the repeatable nature of the proj-
ects, it is also possible to transfer knowledge gained to future
projects. The findings of these case studies and analyses of addi-
tional cases are also expected to provide guidance for analyzing
client behavior in collaborative project environments.
