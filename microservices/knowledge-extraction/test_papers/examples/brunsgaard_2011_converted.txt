One way to save energy is to build houses that satisfy
the German Passive House Standard [2]. A passive house
is usually very well insulated, making it draught-free; the
majority of its windows are orientated towards the sun;
and it has a ventilation system with very efficient heat
recovery. In combination these effects lead to low levels of
energy use. This concept is now acknowledged inter-
nationally and many countries are constructing houses
that meet the Passive House Standard that is defined by
the Passive House Institute in Darmstadt, Germany [2].
The results of a case study on some of the first certified
passive houses in Denmark, called Comfort Houses, are
presented in this paper [3,4]. Comfort Houses exist in 10
different styles of single-family homes. They vary accord-
ing to size, construction materials, style, construction
technique, installations, equipment, etc. Each house was
designed by a different cross-disciplinary design team and
subsequently built to form an estate in an area that is very
quiet and has small amounts of traffic. Eight of the houses
are currently certified as being passive houses and they are
shown in Figure 1. In addition to fulfilling the criteria for a
passive house, the Comfort Houses were developed with a
focus on creating a comfortable indoor environment. The
vision of the designers was to make people aware that
passive houses are synonymous with comfortable houses.
The designers had two key objectives. First, they wanted
to highlight the positive consequences of constructing
passive houses. The basic design automatically fulfils some
comfort aspects such as warm indoor temperatures and
exclusion of draughts due to the creation of an insulated
and airtight building envelope while maintaining air
quality using a mechanical ventilation system. Second,
they wanted to highlight the comfort aspect of their new
designs. It is thought that comfort level will be a key

evaluated using both quantitative measurements and

more complete and holistic picture of the indoor environ-

will be outlined and further discussed with a more detailed
explanation of the qualitative and quantitative methods.

Mixed Methods
The use of mixed methods, that integrates qualitative
and quantitative techniques, has become increasingly
popular since the 1980s [6]. There is an extensive literature
on the use of mixed methods in social research, for
example, Bryman [6] and Tashakkori and Teddlie [7].
According to Bryman [6] there are several reasons for
using mixed methods and he developed different categories
of mixed-method approaches e.g. triangulation, complete-
ness, explanation, credibility, illustration, utility, enhance-
ment, etc [6,8]. This paper will not outline the meaning
of all these approaches; it will, however, illustrate the case
for combining both qualitative and quantitative research
in different ways. To answer the research question
considered in this study, mixed methods are essential.

Thermal Comfort and Indoor Air Quality
The thermal comfort and indoor air quality were
evaluated using the guidelines set out in the European
criteria for the indoor environment, CR 1752 which were
developed for office buildings [11]. Therefore, they can
only be used as an indicator because, first, the comfort
levels at work and at home vary due to different activities
and clothing levels, and second, the indoor environment of
a home is a person-specific concept, i.e. each individual
has his/her idea of what constitutes a perfect environment.
This also underlines the necessity to combine the meas-
urements with qualitative interviews. Since the houses are
marketed as Comfort Houses, it seems reasonable to
expect that the houses should meet category B as a
minimum with regard to CR 1752. The requirement for
the thermal indoor climate therefore is a temperature in
the summer period of 24  1.58C and in the winter period
22  28C.
Regarding the indoor atmospheric environment the
CO2 level in the houses must not exceed 660 ppm above
the outdoor level (outdoor average is 370 ppm), i.e.
1030 ppm, to meet category B. With regard to RH,
CR1752 recommends a range between 30 and 70%. An
RH lower than 30% may result in discomfort in the form
of dry air, static electricity and desiccated mucosa. The
upper limit of 70% should be respected to avoid problems
with moisture and mould in the dwelling.
The acceptable length of the time period required
should be discussed if it deviates from the specified
category. For this purpose the recommendations in the
European Standard, EN 15251, which followed up on CR
1752, can be used [12]. Here it is suggested that deviations
from a category should not exceed more than 3–5% of the
time. A 5% variation in the three summer months
corresponds to 110 h, which was the target value used in
the work reported in this paper.

Quantitative Measurements of the Indoor Environment
Even though the designers wanted to focus on comfort,
the tender document for the Comfort Houses did not
contain specific demands for the indoor environment.
Therefore, evaluation criteria of a comfortable indoor
environment in the measurement project needed to be
defined. They are presented in the following discussion.
A distinction is made between continuous measure-
ments and spot measurements. The first were made every
5 min throughout the measurement period (3 years). The
equipment was placed centrally in one bedroom, the
master bedroom (only in some cases), living room, kitchen
and bathroom at a height of 0.16 m. The parameters for
continuous measurements were: temperature, CO2 level
and relative humidity (RH). In this study the focus of the
measurements was on the living room and bedroom. In
some analyses other rooms are used to illustrate simila-
rities or differences. The bathroom measurements will not
be included in this paper. The spot measurements were
made during visits to the houses. They cover daylight

Noise
The evaluation of the measurements of noise from the
ventilation system and the reverberation times in the living
room were based on Danish Standard DS490 [15] since
BR08 refers to the function requirements in this standard.
Even though the demands in BR08 are a minimum of class
C, the demand in this evaluation was set to class B,
because of the formulation of the demands in the tender
document:

If the house is to be a comfortable place in
which to live consideration must be given in the
project to the building’s noise levels. In par-
ticular the level of reverberation should be
taken into account. Also, all construction
joints, installations and penetrations must be
soundproofed. (translated from the Danish)

This formulation equals the formulation of class B in
Danish Standard DS490, as referred to in the Building
Regulations (BR08), Denmark. It is required that the noise
from the ventilation system does not exceed 25 dB during
normal operation and the reverberation time must not be
longer than 0.6 s in a furnished room [15]. All meas-
urements were performed in the living and dining rooms,
but without furniture, because the occupants had not
moved in at the time of the measurements. This will of
course to be taken into account when assessing the
obtained results.

Discussion

Occupant: No, it is just perfect. (occupant,

Overall, the study shows that the occupants’ experi-
ences are consistent with the measured data, and in about
half of the topics investigated the requirements to meet
category B are fulfilled, see Table 7 for an overview. The
concept of passive houses is new to the building industry in
Denmark and the pilot project of the Comfort Houses is
one of the first attempts to manufacture this type of house.
That those three cases are not totally successful in creating
an outstanding comfort level does not mean that it is a bad
idea to construct passive or low-energy houses. What has
been learned from these pilot projects can be used to
improve the next generation of passive and low-energy
houses. The results from this project form part of the
analyses carried out for the Danish Enterprise and
Construction Authority in preparation for the Danish
low-energy class 2020 [21], which was announced during
August 2011. The measurement project continues to run
until 2012 and will include the other five Comfort Houses
that were not considered in this paper; this will generate
even more knowledge about this field.
The barriers to fulfilling the criteria in category B lie in
the conflicting nature of some of the investigated topics,
e.g. the CO2 level and the humidity which can be difficult
to fulfil at the same time as seen in the winter period in this
study. Additionally, some of the approaches to energy
savings conflict with the indoor environment, e.g. large
windows facing to the south, which deliver passive solar
heat gains in winter, can be a problem in the summer and
may result in excess temperatures if no precautions are
taken.

Second, the occupants have not been fully informed

necessary if the indoor environments in offices are to be

The fact that the European planning criteria in CR 1752
[11] (developed for offices buildings) were used to evaluate
the indoor environment in houses means that caution has
to be taken in drawing conclusions from the presented
results. The comfort level in a house is often a result of the
desires and resulting choices of the occupants of that
house, e.g. one person would like a temperature of 208C in
the living room whereas others would prefer 238C or one
person a colder bedroom than their partner.
One key advantage of the current study is the combined
use of measured data and interviews; the latter proving to
be highly helpful in explaining some of the measured
conditions. It should be noted that it is possible that the
occupants’ perceptions could be influenced by the meas-
uring equipment, which was located on the walls at a
height of 0.16 m from the floor and therefore highly visible
to the occupants. The equipment has a display showing the
actual value of the measured data, therefore one could
imagine that if an occupant, for example, reads 19.58C on
the measuring device, s/he could become aware that the
temperature was below 208C, and this could therefore lead
to them to express a feeling of being cold, although this
should not be the case. Despite this, the occupants’
opinions are still important for a more detailed picture of
the indoor environment, since measurements can usually
only say that a situation exists, whereas interviews can
explain why this situation has occurred.
It is clear that design-related problems occur in these
houses, and that these are a result of decisions made
during the design process by the design team, which in
these cases were different cross-disciplinary design teams.
Two factors can be identified that have an influence on the
design decisions. First, the demands for comfort in the
Comfort Houses and house occupancy were not fully
clearly specified in the tender document and therefore the
design teams defined individual demands for comfort and
periods of occupancy, which in most cases resulted in
decisions being made that were based on previous
estimated solutions [19]. It is not a regular practice in
the building industry to document single-family houses in
such detail as the level presented in this study. However, if
Comfort Houses are to become a viable part of the
housing mix in the future, then the building industry needs
to change its practice and closely document the indoor
environmental aspects of these houses, e.g. an hour-by-
hour dynamic simulation of the indoor temperature or
calculation of the reverberation time instead of selecting
standard solutions based on previous experience. Passive
houses or Comfort Houses are not yet off-the-shelf items.

operational guide for the ventilation or air-
conditioning system and it shall be stated
that
the
indoor

The building concepts need to be more robust so that
